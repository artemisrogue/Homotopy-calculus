## 1. Foundations: Goodwillie calculus (Calculus I-III)

Goodwillie calculus — the calculus of homotopy functors — was created by Thomas Goodwillie in a trilogy of papers: Calculus I, "The first derivative of pseudoisotopy theory" (K-Theory 4 (1990), 1–27); Calculus II, "Analytic functors" (K-Theory 5 (1991/92), no. 4, 295–332); and Calculus III, "Taylor series" (Geometry & Topology 7 (2003), 645–711). The program was announced in Goodwillie's ICM address (Kyoto 1990, Proceedings vol. I, 621–630), and the results of Calculus III circulated in preprint form for roughly a decade before publication — a fact worth flagging when reading citations from the 1990s.

The objects of study are homotopy functors: functors F between categories of spaces and/or spectra that preserve weak equivalences. The organizing analogy is with the differential calculus of functions: the role of degree-≤n polynomials is played by n-excisive functors. An (n+1)-cube in a category is a functor from the poset of subsets of an (n+1)-element set; it is cartesian if the initial vertex maps by an equivalence to the homotopy limit of the rest, and strongly cocartesian if every 2-dimensional face is a homotopy pushout. F is n-excisive if it carries every strongly cocartesian (n+1)-cube to a cartesian cube. For n = 0 this says F is homotopy-constant; for n = 1 it is the excision axiom of homology reborn: homotopy pushouts go to homotopy pullbacks. A reduced (F(∗) ≃ ∗) 1-excisive functor is called linear, and a finitary (filtered-colimit-preserving) reduced linear functor from pointed spaces to pointed spaces is of the form X ↦ Ω^∞(E ∧ X) for a spectrum E — the precise sense in which "linear functors are spectra." Indeed, in Lurie's ∞-categorical reworking (Higher Algebra, §1.4.2 and Ch. 6), spectra are defined as reduced excisive functors from finite pointed spaces to spaces.

Calculus I (1990) introduced the first derivative ∂F(X) of a functor at a space X, a spectrum measuring the linearized behavior of F near X, together with a comparison principle: a map of (stably excisive) functors that induces equivalences of derivatives and an equivalence at one value is an equivalence in a range. The headline computation identifies the derivative of Waldhausen's algebraic K-theory of spaces: ∂_Y A ≃ Σ^∞(ΩY)₊. Via Waldhausen's splitting A(X) ≃ Σ^∞X₊ × Wh^Diff(X) and the equivalence 𝒫(X) ≃ Ω²Wh^Diff(X), this computes the first derivative of stable smooth pseudoisotopy theory 𝒫 and identifies, for a map Y → X, the fiber of 𝒫(Y) → 𝒫(X) in a range roughly twice the connectivity of the map.

Calculus II (1991/92) supplies the quantitative machinery. The key definition is stable n-excision: F satisfies E_n(c, κ) if for every strongly cocartesian (n+1)-cube 𝒳 whose initial maps 𝒳(∅) → 𝒳({s}) are k_s-connected with k_s ≥ κ, the cube F(𝒳) is (−c + Σ_s k_s)-cartesian. F is ρ-analytic if there is a constant q with F satisfying E_n(nρ − q, ρ + 1) for all n ≥ 1 — connectivity estimates linear in n with slope ρ, the analogue of a radius of convergence. The engine behind all examples is Goodwillie's higher Blakers–Massey theorem (Calc II, Theorem 2.3, with a dual in 2.6): a strongly cocartesian n-cube whose initial maps are k_s-connected is (1 − n + Σ_s k_s)-cartesian. Consequences: the identity functor on spaces is 1-analytic (the n = 2 case recovers the classical Blakers–Massey theorem and hence Freudenthal suspension), and Waldhausen's A-theory is 1-analytic.

Calculus III (2003) constructs the Taylor tower. For a set of n+1 points, T_nF(X) := holim over nonempty subsets U ⊆ {0,…,n} of F(X ∗ U), where ∗ is the join; there is a natural map t_nF : F → T_nF, and P_nF := hocolim(F → T_nF → T_n²F → ⋯). Theorem 1.8 of Calc III proves that P_nF is n-excisive and that p_nF : F → P_nF is the universal map (up to homotopy) from F to an n-excisive functor; the crucial step is Lemma 1.9 ("strongly cocartesian cubes are eventually cartesianized by T_n"), for which Rezk (Algebr. Geom. Topol. 13 (2013), 1049–1051) gave a streamlined proof. P_n is an idempotent, localization-type approximation: if F is already n-excisive, p_nF is an equivalence, and ∞-categorically P_n is the left adjoint to the inclusion of n-excisive functors (Lurie, Higher Algebra, Theorem 6.1.1.10). Since (n−1)-excisive implies n-excisive, universality yields maps q_nF : P_nF → P_{n−1}F assembling the Taylor tower F → ⋯ → P_nF → P_{n−1}F → ⋯ → P_0F ≃ F(∗). Its layers D_nF := hofib(P_nF → P_{n−1}F) are n-homogeneous: n-excisive with trivial (n−1)-excisive part. Goodwillie's classification (Calc III, Theorem 3.5 and §5, using cross-effects cr_nF(X₁,…,X_n) — the total homotopy fiber of the cube S ↦ F(⋁_{i∉S} X_i), a notion going back to Eilenberg–Mac Lane — and symmetric multilinear functors) states that finitary n-homogeneous functors correspond to spectra with Σ_n-action: there is a spectrum ∂_nF (the n-th derivative of F at ∗) with Σ_n-action and a natural equivalence D_nF(X) ≃ Ω^∞((∂_nF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣ_n}); homogeneous space-valued functors canonically deloop. Homotopy orbits by Σ_n play the role of dividing by n!. Finally, Theorem 1.13 of Calc III proves convergence: if F is ρ-analytic and X is ρ-connected, the connectivity of F(X) → P_nF(X) grows linearly in n, so F(X) ≃ holim_n P_nF(X). For the identity functor (ρ = 1) the tower converges on simply connected spaces; P_1(Id) ≃ Ω^∞Σ^∞ with ∂_1Id ≃ 𝕊, so the tower interpolates between stable and unstable homotopy theory. Johnson (Trans. AMS 347 (1995), 1295–1321) identified ∂_nId — nonequivariantly a wedge of (n−1)! copies of 𝕊^{1−n}, equivariantly the dual of the partition poset complex — and Arone–Mahowald (Invent. Math. 135 (1999), 743–788) proved that for spheres only prime-power layers are nontrivial and the tower is v_k-periodically finite. Ching (Geom. Topol. 9 (2005), 833–934) exhibited the operad structure on ∂_∗Id inducing the Lie operad in homology. Arone's model for the tower of Σ^∞Map(K, X) (Trans. AMS 351 (1999), 1123–1150) and the tower of Σ^∞Ω^∞, whose layers are Y ↦ (Y^∧n)_{hΣ_n} and whose splitting recovers the Snaith splitting, are the other fundamental worked examples. The standard modern surveys are Arone–Ching's Handbook of Homotopy Theory chapter (2020) and Kuhn's chromatic overview (Geom. Topol. Monogr. 10 (2007), 245–279); Munson–Volić's Cubical Homotopy Theory (2015) is the textbook treatment of the cubical underpinnings.

### Key results

- **First derivative of pseudoisotopy theory / of A-theory.** The functor A (Waldhausen's algebraic K-theory of spaces) has first derivative at any space Y given by ∂_Y A ≃ Σ^∞(ΩY)₊. Via Waldhausen's splitting A(X) ≃ Σ^∞X₊ × Wh^Diff(X) and 𝒫(X) ≃ Ω²Wh^Diff(X), this computes the first derivative of stable smooth pseudoisotopy theory 𝒫 and identifies, for a map Y → X, the homotopy fiber of 𝒫(Y) → 𝒫(X) in a range roughly twice the connectivity of the map. The paper also proves the comparison principle: a map of stably excisive functors inducing equivalences on all first derivatives, and an equivalence at one value, is an equivalence in a range.  
  *T. G. Goodwillie, Calculus I, K-Theory 4 (1990), no. 1, 1–27.*
- **Higher Blakers–Massey theorem.** If 𝒳 is a strongly cocartesian n-cube (n ≥ 2) such that each initial map 𝒳(∅) → 𝒳({s}) is k_s-connected, then 𝒳 is (1 − n + Σ_s k_s)-cartesian. (For n = 2 this is the classical Blakers–Massey triad connectivity theorem; there is a dual version, Calc II Thm 2.6, for strongly cartesian cubes.) Corollaries: the identity functor on spaces is 1-analytic, and Freudenthal suspension follows from the n = 2 case.  
  *T. G. Goodwillie, Calculus II, Theorem 2.3 (dual: 2.6), K-Theory 5 (1991/92), no. 4, 295–332.*
- **Analyticity of A-theory.** Waldhausen's functor A (and hence stable pseudoisotopy theory) is 1-analytic: it satisfies the stable excision estimates Eₙ(n − q, 2) for all n ≥ 1. Combined with the Calc I comparison principle this yields the identification of relative pseudoisotopy theory in the concordance-stable range.  
  *T. G. Goodwillie, Calculus II, §4, K-Theory 5 (1991/92), 295–332.*
- **Existence and universality of the n-excisive approximation.** For any homotopy functor F, the functor PₙF = hocolim(F → TₙF → Tₙ²F → ⋯) is n-excisive, and pₙF: F → PₙF is universal (initial up to homotopy) among natural transformations from F to n-excisive functors. Pₙ is idempotent: pₙ(PₙF) is an equivalence, so Pₙ is a localization onto the subcategory of n-excisive functors. Key step: Lemma 1.9 (Tₙ cartesianizes strongly cocartesian cubes), later given a streamlined proof by Rezk. ∞-categorically: Excⁿ(𝒞,𝒟) ⊆ Fun(𝒞,𝒟) admits a left adjoint Pₙ.  
  *T. G. Goodwillie, Calculus III, Theorem 1.8 and Lemma 1.9, Geom. Topol. 7 (2003), 645–711; C. Rezk, Algebr. Geom. Topol. 13 (2013), 1049–1051; J. Lurie, Higher Algebra, Theorem 6.1.1.10 (2017).*
- **Classification of n-homogeneous functors.** Let F be a finitary n-homogeneous functor from pointed spaces (or spectra) to pointed spaces (or spectra). Then there is a spectrum ∂ₙF with Σₙ-action and a natural equivalence F(X) ≃ Ω^∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}) (omit Ω^∞ for spectrum-valued F). This gives an equivalence between the homotopy theory of finitary n-homogeneous functors and that of spectra with Σₙ-action; n-homogeneous functors correspond to symmetric multilinear functors of n variables via cross-effects, and every homogeneous space-valued functor deloops canonically to a spectrum-valued one. In particular the layers of any Taylor tower satisfy DₙF(X) ≃ Ω^∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}).  
  *T. G. Goodwillie, Calculus III, Theorem 3.5 and §5, Geom. Topol. 7 (2003), 645–711.*
- **Convergence of the Taylor tower for analytic functors.** If F is ρ-analytic (with excision constant q), then for k-connected X with k ≥ ρ the connectivity of F(X) → PₙF(X) grows linearly in n (with slope governed by k − ρ), and for ρ-connected X the map F(X) → holim_n PₙF(X) is a weak equivalence: the Taylor tower converges. Example: the tower of the identity functor (ρ = 1) converges on simply connected spaces; the tower of A-theory converges on simply connected spaces.  
  *T. G. Goodwillie, Calculus III, Theorem 1.13, Geom. Topol. 7 (2003), 645–711.*
- **Linear functors are spectra; P₁ of the identity.** For reduced finitary F: Top∗ → Top∗, P₁F(X) ≃ hocolim_n ΩⁿF(ΣⁿX), and a reduced finitary 1-excisive functor has the form X ↦ Ω^∞(E ∧ X) for a spectrum E ≃ ∂₁F. Consequently P₁(Id) ≃ Ω^∞Σ^∞ = Q, and ∂₁Id ≃ 𝕊, the sphere spectrum: stabilization is the best linear approximation of the identity, and the unit X → Ω^∞Σ^∞X is the functor-calculus linearization map. In Lurie's formulation, Sp ≃ Exc∗(𝒮^fin∗, 𝒮).  
  *T. G. Goodwillie, Calculus I (1990) and Calculus III §2 (2003); J. Lurie, Higher Algebra §1.4.2 (2017).*
- **Derivatives of the identity functor.** ∂ₙId is, Σₙ-equivariantly, the Spanier–Whitehead dual of the (suspended) partition poset complex; nonequivariantly ∂ₙId ≃ ⋁_{(n−1)!} 𝕊^{1−n}, a wedge of (n−1)! copies of the (1−n)-sphere spectrum. For X = S^{2m+1} an odd sphere, the layers DₙId(X) are contractible unless n = p^k is a prime power, and the tower is finite in v_k-periodic homotopy; localized at a prime p, only layers n = p^k contribute to the p-local tower of spheres. The derivatives ∂∗Id form an operad whose homology is the Lie operad (shifted).  
  *B. Johnson, Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 347 (1995), 1295–1321; G. Arone and M. Mahowald, Invent. Math. 135 (1999), 743–788; M. Ching, Geom. Topol. 9 (2005), 833–934.*
- **Taylor tower of Σ^∞Ω^∞ and the Snaith splitting.** The functor Σ^∞Ω^∞: Sp → Sp has n-th layer Dₙ(Σ^∞Ω^∞)(Y) ≃ (Y^∧n)_{hΣₙ}, i.e. ∂ₙ(Σ^∞Ω^∞) ≃ 𝕊 with trivial Σₙ-action. For 0-connected Y the tower splits: Σ^∞Ω^∞Y ≃ ⋁_{n≥1} (Y^∧n)_{hΣₙ}, recovering (for Y = Σ^∞X) the stable splitting of QX = Ω^∞Σ^∞X due to Snaith (1974) and Kahn. Arone's model gives the analogous tower for X ↦ Σ^∞Map(K, X), converging when conn(X) ≥ dim K.  
  *Tower interpretation: T. G. Goodwillie, Calculus III (2003); G. Arone, Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 351 (1999), 1123–1150; survey treatment in N. Kuhn, Geom. Topol. Monogr. 10 (2007), 245–279.*

### Worked examples suitable for teaching

- Computing T₁ and P₁ by hand. For n = 1, the nonempty subsets of {0,1} give T₁F(X) = holim( F(X ∗ {0}) → F(X ∗ {0,1}) ← F(X ∗ {1}) ). Since X ∗ {pt} = CX ≃ ∗ and X ∗ S⁰ = ΣX, for reduced F this is holim(∗ → F(ΣX) ← ∗) ≃ ΩF(ΣX). Iterating: P₁F(X) ≃ hocolim_n ΩⁿF(ΣⁿX). Taking F = Id gives P₁Id(X) ≃ hocolim ΩⁿΣⁿX = QX = Ω^∞Σ^∞X, so the linearization of the identity is stable homotopy and ∂₁Id = 𝕊.
- Freudenthal from higher Blakers–Massey. Let X be k-connected and consider the strongly cocartesian square X → CX, X → CX with pushout ΣX. Both initial maps X → CX ≃ ∗ are (k+1)-connected, so the square is (1 − 2 + 2(k+1)) = (2k+1)-cartesian: X → holim(∗ → ΣX ← ∗) = ΩΣX is (2k+1)-connected. This is exactly the Freudenthal suspension theorem, and it is the first manifestation of the 1-analyticity of the identity functor.
- Second cross-effect of the identity. cr₂Id(X, Y) = hofib(X ∨ Y → X × Y). By Hilton–Milnor this fiber is built from smash products (its first interesting piece is Σ(ΩX ∧ ΩY), sourcing Whitehead products). Multilinearizing gives ∂₂Id ≃ 𝕊^{−1} (one copy, (2−1)! = 1), so D₂Id(X) ≃ Ω^∞(Σ^{−1}(X ∧ X)_{hΣ₂}). For X = Sⁿ the Σ₂-homotopy orbits produce a stunted real projective spectrum, connecting the quadratic layer to the EHP sequence (Behrens).
- The tower of the identity at spheres. The fiber sequence D₂Id(X) → P₂Id(X) → QX shows how P₂ interpolates between unstable and stable homotopy. By Arone–Mahowald, for an odd sphere the only nontrivial layers are at n = p^k; p-locally the tower of S^{2m+1} has finitely many nonzero layers in v_k-periodic homotopy, giving a functor-calculus proof of exponents and a bridge to chromatic homotopy theory.
- Snaith splitting as a split Taylor tower. For F = Σ^∞Ω^∞: Sp → Sp the layers are Dₙ F(Y) ≃ (Y^∧n)_{hΣₙ} and the tower splits for connected Y: Σ^∞QX ≃ ⋁ₙ Σ^∞(EΣₙ₊ ∧_{Σₙ} X^∧n). Students can compare with the classical configuration-space splittings; Arone's Trans. AMS 1999 model does the same for Σ^∞Map(K, −), converging when conn(X) ≥ dim K.
- Bookkeeping with analyticity constants. The identity is 1-analytic with q = 1 (it satisfies Eₙ(n − 1, 2) via higher Blakers–Massey). Hence for a 1-connected (simply connected) X the maps Id(X) → PₙId(X) become highly connected and X ≃ holim PₙId(X). A-theory is also 1-analytic, so A(X) is recovered from its tower on simply connected X; combined with ∂_Y A ≃ Σ^∞(ΩY)₊ this is the engine of Goodwillie's pseudoisotopy applications.

### Caveats and misconception traps

- The cubes in the definition of n-excisive must be STRONGLY cocartesian (all 2-faces pushouts), not merely cocartesian (colimit condition on the total cube); requiring only cocartesian cubes gives a wrong, much stronger condition.
- Convergence of the Taylor tower is a theorem with hypotheses, never automatic: it requires ρ-analyticity of F AND ρ-connectivity of the input (Calc III Thm 1.13). E.g. the identity tower is only guaranteed to converge on simply connected spaces (extensions to nilpotent spaces are separate, later results — do not silently upgrade).
- The classification DₙF(X) ≃ Ω^∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}) requires F finitary (filtered-colimit-preserving); without this hypothesis homogeneous functors need not come from spectra with Σₙ-action.
- It is homotopy ORBITS (−)_{hΣₙ}, not fixed points, in the layer formula — the homotopy-theoretic 'division by n!'; the two differ integrally (they agree rationally via the norm map).
- Citation dates are a trap: Calculus II is K-Theory 5 (1991/92) — cited as both 1991 and 1992 — and Calculus III was published in 2003 but circulated in preprint form since the early 1990s, so papers from the 1990s cite it as a preprint.
- Goodwillie calculus (homotopy functor calculus) must not be conflated with manifold/embedding calculus (Goodwillie–Weiss) or orthogonal calculus (Weiss); all three share the Taylor-tower metaphor but have different inputs and towers.
- The formula P₁F(X) ≃ hocolim ΩⁿF(ΣⁿX) is valid for REDUCED functors; for unreduced F, P₀F ≃ F(∗) is nontrivial and P₁ mixes in the constant term. Similarly 'linear' means reduced AND 1-excisive — 1-excisive alone allows an affine constant part.
- ρ-analyticity is a system of connectivity estimates (stable n-excision uniform in n with slope ρ), a metaphor for — not an instance of — numerical convergence of series; the constant q matters for the range in comparison theorems.
- Taylor towers rarely split: the Snaith splitting for Σ^∞Ω^∞ is exceptional. In general the tower has nontrivial extensions (k-invariants), which is exactly what makes it a computational tool (spectral sequences) rather than a decomposition.
- Calc I's derivative ∂F(X) at a space X and Calc III's derivatives ∂ₙF (Taylor coefficients at ∗) are related but distinct notions; 'the derivative of A-theory' ∂_Y A ≃ Σ^∞(ΩY)₊ is a Calc I statement about expansion at an arbitrary Y.
- Vanishing of the (n+1)-st cross-effect ('degree ≤ n' in the Eilenberg–Mac Lane/Johnson–McCarthy cotriple sense) is implied by n-excision but is in general weaker for space-valued functors; the cotriple tower and Goodwillie tower agree only under stability-type hypotheses.
- Everything stated here (classification, convergence, Blakers–Massey, A-theory analyticity) is proved theorem, not conjecture; but beware that some folklore statements (e.g. convergence of the identity tower beyond simply connected spaces, chain rules, operadic structure) belong to later literature (Arone–Ching, Ching, Behrens, Heuts), not to Calc I–III themselves.

- T₁-iteration animation: show F(X) → ΩF(ΣX) → Ω²F(Σ²X) → ⋯ stabilizing; for F = Id plot πₖ(ΩⁿΣⁿSᵐ) against n and watch Freudenthal stabilization produce πₖˢ — makes 'P₁Id = Ω^∞Σ^∞' computational.
- Convergence calculator: inputs ρ (analyticity), q (excision constant), conn(X); output a plot of the guaranteed connectivity of F(X) → PₙF(X) versus n, with a red/green banner for whether Thm 1.13 applies (X ρ-connected); preset buttons for Id (ρ = 1) and A (ρ = 1).
- Ordinary-calculus dictionary as flip cards: each card shows the calculus notion (Taylor polynomial, coefficient, n!, radius of convergence, mean value theorem) and flips to the functor-calculus counterpart with the precise definition and citation.
- Partition complex gallery: draw the partition posets for n = 2, 3, 4 with the Σₙ-action, and state ∂ₙId ≃ dual of the suspended partition complex ≃ ⋁_{(n−1)!} 𝕊^{1−n} nonequivariantly; link forward to the Lie operad (Ching).
- Exercise set (collapsibles): (a) prove a 0-excisive functor is homotopy constant; (b) show a reduced 1-excisive functor satisfies F(X) ≃ ΩF(ΣX); (c) compute T₁F(X) ≃ ΩF(ΣX) from the join formula; (d) derive Freudenthal from higher Blakers–Massey; (e) deduce idempotency of Pₙ from the universal property; (f) compute cr₂Id(Sᵖ, Sᵠ) through dimension p+q+1 via Hilton–Milnor.
- Snaith splitting demo: for X = S¹, list the wedge summands Σ^∞(EΣₙ₊ ∧_{Σₙ} (S¹)^∧n) of Σ^∞QS¹ and their low-dimensional homology, showing the tower of Σ^∞Ω^∞ splitting piece by piece.

## 2. The dictionary with differential calculus, and polynomial approximation as sheafification

Goodwillie&rsquo;s calculus of homotopy functors, developed in the trilogy Calculus I–III (K-Theory 4 (1990) 1–27; K-Theory 5 (1991/92) 295–332; Geometry & Topology 7 (2003) 645–711), organizes homotopy theory around a systematic analogy with differential calculus. A homotopy functor F: Top∗ → Top∗ — more generally F: C → D with C admitting finite homotopy colimits and a terminal object and D "differentiable" — plays the role of a smooth function; preservation of weak equivalences is the analogue of continuity. The polynomial hierarchy is the excision hierarchy: F is n-excisive if it sends strongly cocartesian (n+1)-cubes to cartesian cubes. Reduced finitary 1-excisive functors are generalized homology theories in disguise (X ↦ Ω∞(E ∧ X)), whence the slogan "homology is the derivative of homotopy". Goodwillie constructs a universal n-excisive approximation: T_nF(X) = holim over nonempty U ⊆ {0,…,n} of F(X ∗ U) (joins with finite sets), and P_nF = hocolim(F → T_nF → T_n²F → ⋯) (Calculus III, Theorem 1.8; Rezk (Algebr. Geom. Topol. 13 (2013) 1049–1051) gave a streamlined proof of the key Lemma 1.9). The Taylor tower ⋯ → P_nF → P_{n−1}F → ⋯ → P_0F ≃ F(∗) is the sequence of Taylor polynomials expanded at the one-point space. Its layers D_nF = hofib(P_nF → P_{n−1}F) are n-homogeneous and are classified by spectra with Σₙ-action: D_nF(X) ≃ Ω∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}). The derivative spectrum ∂ₙF is the coefficient f⁽ⁿ⁾(0), and homotopy orbits (−)_{hΣₙ} are the homotopical residue of dividing by n!; cross-effects play the role of finite differences.

Convergence is governed by analyticity. Calculus II introduces the stable excision estimates Eₙ(c,κ) and calls F ρ-analytic if for some fixed q it satisfies Eₙ(nρ−q, ρ+1) for every n. Theorem (Goodwillie 1991/92, 2003): if F is ρ-analytic and X is ρ-connected, then F(X) → holim_n P_nF(X) is an equivalence, the connectivity of F(X) → P_nF(X) growing linearly in n. Thus ρ is a radius of convergence and connectivity of X measures distance from the expansion point ∗. The identity functor is 1-analytic — a repackaging of the higher-dimensional Blakers–Massey theorem — so its tower converges on simply connected spaces; ∂ₙId is the Σₙ-equivariant Spanier–Whitehead dual of the partition poset complex, with underlying spectrum a wedge of (n−1)! copies of S^(1−n) (Johnson, Trans. AMS 347 (1995) 1295–1321; Arone–Mahowald, Invent. Math. 135 (1999) 743–788, who used the tower on spheres to study vₙ-periodic homotopy). Σ∞Ω∞ is 0-analytic with D_n(Σ∞Ω∞)(X) ≃ Σ∞(X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}: the tower recovers the Snaith splitting.

The sheafification story has three strands. (a) Formal: P_n is idempotent, and F → P_nF is universal among maps to n-excisive functors. In Lurie&rsquo;s Higher Algebra (Theorem 6.1.1.10): if C has finite colimits and a terminal object and D is differentiable (finite limits and sequential colimits exist and commute), the inclusion Exc^n(C,D) ⊆ Fun(C,D) admits a left adjoint P_n which preserves finite limits; for presentable D this is an accessible left exact localization — exactly the formal shape of sheafification a: PSh(C) → Sh(C,J), itself an accessible left exact localization/idempotent monad. Even the constructions run parallel: T_n is a one-step "plus construction" and P_n = colim T_nᵏ mirrors sheafification by iterated plus construction. Anel–Biedermann–Finster–Joyal (J. Topology 11 (2018) 1100–1132) push this furthest: the n-excisive localizations are left exact modalities on functor ∞-topoi (so ∞-categories of n-excisive functors are ∞-topoi), and they prove a Blakers–Massey theorem for the Goodwillie tower — a pushout of a P_m-equivalence against a P_n-equivalence has P_{m+n+1}-cartesian gap — from which delooping of homogeneous functors follows. Crucially, these localizations need not be topological (generated by monomorphisms), so in homotopy calculus "P_n = sheafification" is an analogy of formal structure, not a literal Grothendieck-topology statement. Model-categorically the same picture appears as left Bousfield localizations (Biedermann–Chorny–Röndigs, Adv. Math. 214 (2007) 92–115), with the n-homogeneous structure Quillen equivalent, on finitary functors, to spectra with Σₙ-action.

(b) In manifold calculus the analogy is a theorem. Weiss (Geom. Topol. 3 (1999) 67–101; erratum Geom. Topol. 15 (2011) 407–409) studies good contravariant functors F: 𝒪(M)ᵒᵖ → Top; F is polynomial of degree ≤ k if for pairwise disjoint closed A₀,…,A_k ⊆ V the (k+1)-cube S ↦ F(V ∖ ⋃_{i∈S} A_i) is cartesian, and T_kF(V) = holim of F over opens in V diffeomorphic to ≤ k balls. Boavida de Brito–Weiss (Homology Homotopy Appl. 15 (2013) 361–383, arXiv:1202.1305) define Grothendieck topologies J_k on the site of manifolds: a J_k-cover is an open cover in which every set of at most k points lies in a single member. Their theorems: F is polynomial of degree ≤ k iff F is a homotopy J_k-sheaf, and F → T_kF is precisely homotopy J_k-sheafification; the Taylor tower is thus a tower of homotopy sheafifications, and T_∞ is homotopy sheafification for the Weiss topology J_∞ (every finite subset in one member), for which disjoint unions of balls form a basis — so T_∞F is determined by the restriction of F to the site of disks, and (in their enriched refinement) the limit of the embedding tower is a derived space of right-module maps over the framed little-discs operad. Since degree ≤ 1 means ordinary homotopy sheaf, linear functors are section spaces of fibrations — the exact analogue of "a linear function is determined by its slope". The jet analogy is also faithful: a degree ≤ k functor is determined by its values on ≤ k balls, as a degree ≤ k polynomial is determined by its k-jet or by k+1 sampled values; and T_1Emb(−,N) ≃ Imm(−,N) by Smale–Hirsch, so immersion theory is literally the linearization of embedding theory (Weiss, Bull. AMS 33 (1996) 177–187). Convergence again needs a radius: by Goodwillie–Weiss (Geom. Topol. 3 (1999) 103–118) together with Goodwillie–Klein multiple disjunction (J. Topology 8 (2015) 651–674), Emb(M,N) → T_kEmb(M,N) is (k(n−m−2)+1−m)-connected; for codimension n−m ≥ 3 the tower converges. Codimension ≥ 3 is the disk of convergence; in codimension 2 (classical knots) convergence is open, though the tower produces finite-type invariants.

(c) The covariant mirror is cosheaf-theoretic: Ayala–Francis (J. Topology 8 (2015) 1045–1084) characterize factorization homology ∫_M A by ⊗-excision (Eilenberg–Steenrod-style axioms), and their Handbook primer (arXiv:1903.10961) frames factorization algebras as Weiss cosheaves — covariant functors satisfying codescent for Weiss covers — with ∫_M A the derived global sections (a colimit): the cosheafification counterpart of Boavida de Brito–Weiss. Recommended surveys: Kuhn (Geom. Topol. Monogr. 10 (2007) 245–279) and Arone–Ching&rsquo;s Handbook chapter (arXiv:1902.00803); Arone–Ching (Astérisque 338, 2011) supply the chain rule (∂∗(F∘G) via a composition product — a homotopical Faà di Bruno formula); Heuts (Mem. AMS no. 1333, 2021) categorifies the tower; Munson–Volić (Cubical Homotopy Theory, CUP 2015) is the textbook treatment of the cubical technology underlying both calculi.

### Key results

- **Existence of the n-excisive approximation (Taylor tower).** Let F: C → D be a homotopy functor, C with finite homotopy colimits and terminal object, D differentiable. Then P_nF = hocolim(F → T_nF → T_n²F → ⋯), with T_nF(X) = holim_{∅≠U⊆{0,…,n}} F(X ∗ U), is n-excisive, and F → P_nF is universal (initial up to homotopy) among natural transformations from F to n-excisive functors. The P_n assemble into the Taylor tower ⋯ → P_nF → P_{n−1}F → ⋯.  
  *T. Goodwillie, Calculus III: Taylor series, Geom. Topol. 7 (2003) 645–711, Theorem 1.8; key Lemma 1.9 streamlined by C. Rezk, Algebr. Geom. Topol. 13 (2013) 1049–1051; ∞-categorical form in J. Lurie, Higher Algebra, §6.1.1.*
- **P_n is a left exact (accessible) localization — the sheafification shape.** If C admits finite colimits and a terminal object and D is differentiable, the inclusion Exc^n(C,D) ⊆ Fun(C,D) admits a left adjoint P_n, and P_n preserves finite limits. When D is presentable this exhibits Exc^n(C,D) as an accessible left exact localization of Fun(C,D) — the same formal structure (idempotent left exact monad) as sheafification a: PSh → Sh.  
  *J. Lurie, Higher Algebra (2017 version), Theorem 6.1.1.10 and Construction 6.1.1.27.*
- **Classification of homogeneous functors.** For finitary F: Top∗ → Top∗ (or to Sp), n-homogeneous functors correspond to spectra with Σₙ-action: there is a spectrum ∂ₙF with Σₙ-action and a natural equivalence D_nF(X) ≃ Ω∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}). In particular every homogeneous space-valued functor is an infinite loop space valued functor (delooping).  
  *T. Goodwillie, Calculus III (2003), §§2–5 (Theorem 3.5 for the spectrum-valued case); refined to a Quillen equivalence on finitary functors by G. Biedermann, B. Chorny, O. Röndigs, Adv. Math. 214 (2007) 92–115.*
- **Convergence theorem (analyticity ⇒ convergence on a disk).** If F is ρ-analytic and X is ρ-connected, then F(X) → holim_n P_nF(X) is an equivalence; more precisely the connectivity of F(X) → P_nF(X) grows linearly in n, with slope controlled by conn(X) − ρ. Analogue: a power series converges to f inside its radius of convergence.  
  *T. Goodwillie, Calculus II: Analytic functors, K-Theory 5 (1991/92) 295–332, together with Calculus III (2003), §1.*
- **The identity functor is 1-analytic.** Id: Top∗ → Top∗ is 1-analytic (a reformulation of the higher-dimensional Blakers–Massey theorems); hence its Taylor tower converges on simply connected spaces. Moreover ∂ₙId ≃ Σₙ-equivariant Spanier–Whitehead dual of the partition poset complex, with underlying spectrum ⋁_{(n−1)!} S^{1−n}.  
  *Analyticity: Goodwillie, Calculus II (1991/92). Derivatives: B. Johnson, Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 347 (1995) 1295–1321; reformulated and exploited by G. Arone, M. Mahowald, Invent. Math. 135 (1999) 743–788.*
- **Manifold-calculus polynomial approximation IS homotopy sheafification.** On the site of smooth m-manifolds (or 𝒪(M)) with the Grothendieck topology J_k whose covers are open covers in which every set of ≤ k points lies in a single member: a good functor F is polynomial of degree ≤ k iff F is a homotopy J_k-sheaf, and the canonical map F → T_kF exhibits T_kF as the homotopy J_k-sheafification of F. Consequently the Taylor tower is a tower of homotopy sheafifications, and T_∞F is homotopy sheafification for the Weiss topology J_∞, for which disjoint unions of at most finitely many balls form a basis (so T_∞F is determined by F restricted to the site of disks). In the enriched setting the tower limit for embedding-type functors is a derived space of right-module maps over the framed little-discs operad.  
  *P. Boavida de Brito, M. Weiss, Manifold calculus and homotopy sheaves, Homology Homotopy Appl. 15 (2013), no. 2, 361–383 (arXiv:1202.1305).*
- **Convergence of embedding calculus (codimension ≥ 3).** For smooth manifolds Mᵐ, Nⁿ, the map Emb(M,N) → T_kEmb(M,N) is (k(n−m−2)+1−m)-connected. Hence if n−m ≥ 3 the connectivities tend to ∞ and Emb(M,N) ≃ holim_k T_kEmb(M,N): the tower converges. Also T_1Emb(−,N) ≃ Imm(−,N) (Smale–Hirsch) when the calculus applies, so immersions are the linearization of embeddings.  
  *T. Goodwillie, M. Weiss, Geom. Topol. 3 (1999) 103–118, relying on the multiple-disjunction results of T. Goodwillie, J. Klein, J. Topology 8 (2015), no. 3, 651–674.*
- **Blakers–Massey theorem for the Goodwillie tower (topos-theoretic P_n).** The classes of P_n-equivalences form left exact modalities on suitable functor ∞-topoi; in particular ∞-categories of n-excisive functors are ∞-topoi. Generalized Blakers–Massey: in a homotopy pushout square in which one leg is a P_m-equivalence and the other a P_n-equivalence, the gap map to the homotopy pullback is a P_{m+n+1}-equivalence. Delooping of homogeneous functors is a corollary. These localizations are left exact but need not be topological (not generated by monomorphisms), marking the precise boundary of the sheafification analogy in homotopy calculus.  
  *M. Anel, G. Biedermann, E. Finster, A. Joyal, Goodwillie’s calculus of functors and higher topos theory, J. Topology 11 (2018) 1100–1132 (arXiv:1703.09632); continued in their Left-exact localizations of ∞-topoi series (Adv. Math. 400 (2022); JPAA 2024).*
- **Factorization homology as a Weiss cosheaf (covariant mirror).** Factorization homology ∫_−A: Mfld_n → C is characterized axiomatically by ⊗-excision and sequential-colimit compatibility (a multiplicative Eilenberg–Steenrod theorem), and satisfies codescent for Weiss covers: n-dimensional factorization algebras are precisely symmetric monoidal functors that are homotopy cosheaves with respect to the Weiss topology, with ∫_M A computed as a colimit (derived global cosections) over embedded disks.  
  *D. Ayala, J. Francis, Factorization homology of topological manifolds, J. Topology 8 (2015), no. 4, 1045–1084 (arXiv:1206.5522); A factorization homology primer, in Handbook of Homotopy Theory, CRC Press 2020 (arXiv:1903.10961).*

### Worked examples suitable for teaching

- Stable homotopy / Snaith splitting: F = Σ∞Ω∞ is 0-analytic with layers D_nF(X) ≃ Σ∞(X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}; on connected X the tower splits, recovering the classical Snaith splitting Σ∞Ω∞Σ∞X ≃ ⋁ₙ Σ∞(X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}. Good first computation: read off ∂ₙ(Σ∞Ω∞) = 𝕊 with trivial Σₙ-action.
- The identity functor: P_1Id ≃ Ω∞Σ∞ ('the derivative of homotopy is stable homotopy'); ∂ₙId is the dual partition complex, a wedge of (n−1)! copies of S^{1−n} with Σₙ-action; Id is 1-analytic so X ≃ holim P_nId(X) for simply connected X. Arone–Mahowald: on spheres the tower has layers concentrated at n = pᵏ after p-localization, giving the vₙ-periodic applications.
- Mapping spaces: F(X) = Σ∞Map(K,X) for a finite complex K is dim(K)-analytic; its tower (Arone’s model) converges for conn(X) ≥ dim K — a functor whose 'radius of convergence' is visibly the dimension of K (see the Arone–Ching Handbook survey).
- Degree ≤ 2 by hand: X ↦ Σ∞(X ∧ X) is 2-excisive and 2-homogeneous with ∂₂ = Σ₂₊ ∧ 𝕊 (free Σ₂-action): compute (Σ₂₊ ∧ 𝕊 ∧ X^∧2)_{hΣ₂} ≃ Σ∞(X ∧ X). Contrast with X ↦ Σ∞(X ∧ X)_{hΣ₂}, whose ∂₂ = 𝕊 with trivial action.
- Manifold calculus, linear = sheaf: a good cofunctor on 𝒪(M) is polynomial of degree ≤ 1 iff it is a homotopy J_1-sheaf (J_1 = all open covers) iff it is equivalent to V ↦ Γ(p; V), sections of a fibration p over M (Weiss 1999). Emb-side: T_1Emb(−,N) ≃ Imm(−,N) by Smale–Hirsch — immersion theory is the linearization of embedding theory.
- Weiss covers concretely: the collection of all opens of M diffeomorphic to disjoint unions of ≤ k balls is a J_k-cover of M and a basis for J_k; the cover of ℝ by all bounded open intervals is a J_1-cover but NOT a J_2-cover (two far-apart points lie in no single interval) — add disjoint unions of two intervals to fix it. This is the k-points test students can run by hand.
- Embedding tower with numbers: for Emb(M,N) with m = dim M, n = dim N, the map to T_k is (k(n−m−2)+1−m)-connected. For long knots ℝ → ℝⁿ: n ≥ 4 gives convergence; n = 3 (classical knots, codimension 2) gives no convergence guarantee, but the tower stages are related to finite-type (Vassiliev) invariants — a good bridge to KnotLab content.

### Caveats and misconception traps

- Notation clash: in Goodwillie (homotopy) calculus T_n is one step of the construction and P_n = colim T_nᵏ is the polynomial approximation; in manifold calculus T_k itself IS the degree-≤ k approximation (a single homotopy limit over 𝒪_k suffices). Do not mix the two uses of T.
- The sheafification statement is a literal theorem ONLY in manifold calculus (Boavida de Brito–Weiss 2013, via the Grothendieck topologies J_k). In homotopy calculus, P_n is an accessible left exact localization (Lurie HA 6.1.1.10) — the same formal shape as sheafification — but it is in general not a topological localization (not generated by monomorphisms), so no Grothendieck topology on the source presents it; ABFJ's work is the correct general framework. Never present 'P_n = sheafification' as a theorem in the homotopy-calculus setting.
- Convergence is never automatic: F(X) ≃ holim P_nF(X) requires BOTH ρ-analyticity of F AND ρ-connectedness of X. Taylor towers of perfectly reasonable functors fail to converge outside the connectivity range, and the limit P_∞F(X) can differ from F(X).
- The identity-functor tower converges on simply connected spaces (1-analyticity); on general spaces the limit need not be X. Be careful not to state unqualified convergence.
- Embedding calculus: the Goodwillie–Weiss/Goodwillie–Klein convergence theorem needs codimension n − m ≥ 3. For classical knots (codimension 2) convergence is an open problem; the relation of the tower to finite-type (Vassiliev) invariants has strong partial results but should be flagged as partly conjectural.
- Analyticity bookkeeping varies: Goodwillie's ρ-analytic involves an auxiliary constant q (F satisfies Eₙ(nρ−q, ρ+1) for all n), and different surveys normalize the connectivity estimates slightly differently. Quote the estimate from a single fixed source (Calc II or the Arone–Ching Handbook chapter).
- The classification D_nF(X) ≃ Ω∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}) as stated needs F finitary (the tight model-category equivalence with Σₙ-spectra is Biedermann–Chorny–Röndigs 2007). Moreover the derivatives ∂ₙF classify only the layers: reconstructing the tower requires extra structure (k-invariants; module structure over ∂∗Id per Arone–Ching 2011).
- Lurie's construction of P_n requires the target to be differentiable (sequential colimits commute with finite limits); P_n preserves finite limits only — it is left exact, not limit-preserving in general.
- Weiss's Part I (Geom. Topol. 3 (1999) 67–101) has a published erratum, Geom. Topol. 15 (2011) 407–409; cite the corrected statements.
- The Weiss topologies get smaller as k grows (every J_{k+1}-cover is a J_k-cover, J_1 = all open covers), so being a J_k-sheaf is a progressively WEAKER descent condition: degree ≤ 1 = ordinary homotopy sheaf, higher degree = weaker descent. Getting this monotonicity backwards is a common error.
- The jet/interpolation analogy (degree ≤ k determined by values on ≤ k balls ↔ k-jet or k+1 sample points) is documented as an analogy in Weiss's surveys, not as a formal jet-theory equivalence; present it as motivation.

- Plotly convergence demo: left panel shows Taylor partial sums of log(1+x) converging on |x| < 1 and diverging beyond; right panel implements Goodwillie's estimate — sliders for ρ (analyticity), q = conn(X), and n, with a bar showing the connectivity of F(X) → P_nF(X) growing like (n+1)(q−ρ); students see 'divergence' the moment q < ρ.

## 3. Landmark examples and computations

Goodwillie calculus assigns to a homotopy functor F (homotopy-invariant, finitary) a Taylor tower F → … → PₙF → Pₙ₋₁F → … → P₁F, where PₙF is the universal n-excisive approximation. The homogeneous layers DₙF = fib(PₙF → Pₙ₋₁F) are classified by spectra ∂ₙF with Σₙ-action via DₙF(X) ≃ Ω^∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}). The theory was developed by T. Goodwillie in Calculus I (K-Theory 4, 1990, 1–27), Calculus II: Analytic functors (K-Theory 5, 1991/92, 295–332) and Calculus III: Taylor series (Geom. Topol. 7, 2003, 645–711), explicitly motivated by pseudoisotopy theory and Waldhausen's algebraic K-theory of spaces A(X): Calc I identifies the fiber of the stable pseudoisotopy map P(X) → P(Y) in a range roughly twice the connectivity of X → Y, with a corollary for A-theory. The subject's landmark computations fall into five clusters.

(1) Derivatives of the identity. Although Id: Top* → Top* looks trivial, it is only linearly so: P₁Id ≃ Ω^∞Σ^∞, and the higher derivatives encode all unstable homotopy theory. B. Johnson (Trans. AMS 347, 1995, 1295–1321) identified ∂ₙ(Id) as the Spanier–Whitehead dual of an explicit finite complex; reformulated by Arone–Mahowald, ∂ₙ(Id) ≃ 𝔻(Σ|Πₙ|⋄) — the dual of the suspended, doubly-pointed partition complex, as spectra with Σₙ-action. Nonequivariantly ∂ₙ(Id) ≃ ⋁_{(n−1)!} S^{1−n} (since |Πₙ| ≃ ⋁_{(n−1)!} S^{n−3}), but no such equivalence is Σₙ-equivariant. Id is 1-analytic (Calc II), so the tower converges on simply connected spaces. Arone–Mahowald (Invent. Math. 135, 1999, 743–788) proved that p-locally, for an odd sphere S^{2m+1}, the layers Dⱼ(S^{2m+1}) are contractible unless j = pᵏ, that the surviving layers are built from the Steinberg-summand spectra L(k) (cf. Arone–Dwyer, Proc. LMS 82, 2001, 229–256, linking partition complexes, Tits buildings and symmetric powers of spheres), and that the tower of Id at S^{2m+1} is finite through the eyes of vₕ-periodic homotopy: D_{pᵏ}(S^{2m+1}) has nontrivial vₕ-periodic homotopy only for h = k. This gave a new proof of a strong form of the Mahowald–Thompson results and is the fundamental bridge from functor calculus to chromatic homotopy. Ching (Geom. Topol. 9, 2005, 833–934) constructed a cooperad structure on the topological bar construction of an operad and, dualizing, an operad structure on ∂_*(Id) — the spectral Lie operad, Koszul dual to the commutative cooperad, whose homology is the shifted Lie operad: H_{1−n}(∂ₙId) ≅ Lie(n). Behrens (Mem. AMS 218, no. 1026, 2012) interlocked the Goodwillie spectral sequence at spheres with the EHP spectral sequence at p = 2, recomputing the 2-primary unstable stems through the Toda range (19-stem) and introducing Dyer–Lashof-like operations from Ching's operad; his companion paper (Algebr. Geom. Topol. 11, 2011, 2453–2475) proved the calculus form of the Whitehead conjecture: the Whitehead sequence contracts the Goodwillie tower of Id at S¹ at p = 2, matching Kuhn's proof of the 2-primary Whitehead conjecture.

(2) Σ^∞Ω^∞. The stabilization functor Σ^∞Ω^∞: Sp → Sp has ∂ₙ(Σ^∞Ω^∞) ≃ 𝕊 with trivial Σₙ-action, so DₙX ≃ (X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}, and the tower converges on 0-connected spectra — the exact analogue of eˣ − 1 = Σ xⁿ/n!. Evaluated on suspension spectra of connected spaces the tower splits, recovering the Snaith splitting Σ^∞Ω^∞Σ^∞Y ≃ ⋁ₙ Σ^∞(EΣₙ)₊ ∧_{Σₙ} Y^∧n (Snaith, J. London Math. Soc. (2) 7, 1974, 577–583, via May's configuration filtration).

(3) Algebraic K-theory. Waldhausen (Ann. of Math. Stud. 113, 1987, 392–417) proved that stable A-theory is stable homotopy — the linearization D₁Ã(X) ≃ Σ^∞X — so all higher-order information sits in Wh^{Diff}. Goodwillie (Ann. of Math. 124, 1986, 347–402) proved relative rational K-theory of a nilpotent extension is relative cyclic homology shifted by one. Dundas–McCarthy (Ann. of Math. 140, 1994, 685–701) proved stable K-theory equals topological Hochschild homology, K^S(R;M) ≃ THH(R;M): THH is the derivative of K-theory in the bimodule direction. McCarthy (Acta Math. 179, 1997, 197–222) proved relative K ≃ relative TC after profinite completion for nilpotent extensions of simplicial rings; Dundas (Acta Math. 179, 1997, 223–242) extended this, and the integral statement for connective ring spectra — if A → B induces a surjection π₀A → π₀B with nilpotent kernel then the square K/TC is homotopy cartesian — is the Dundas–Goodwillie–McCarthy theorem (Springer, Algebra and Applications 18, 2013); scheme-theoretically, K and TC agree relatively along nilpotent immersions. This is the archetype of "two functors with the same derivative differ by a locally constant functor."

(4) Chromatic interaction. Kuhn (Invent. Math. 157, 2004, 345–370) proved that Tate constructions of finite-group actions vanish after telescopic localization, hence every Taylor tower of a functor Sp → Sp splits T(n)-locally: L_{T(n)}P_dF(X) ≃ ∏_{j≤d} L_{T(n)}DⱼF(X). Applied to Σ^∞Ω^∞ on 0-connected X this is the Kuhn splitting L_{T(n)}Σ^∞Ω^∞X ≃ ∏ⱼ L_{T(n)}(X^∧j)_{hΣⱼ}; his Adv. Math. 201 (2006), 318–378 paper develops the K(n)-homology of infinite loop spaces from this, and his survey (Geom. Topol. Monogr. 10, 2007, 245–279; arXiv math/0410342) is the standard overview. Heuts (Ann. of Math. 193, 2021, 223–301) capped this line: vₙ-periodic unstable homotopy theory is equivalent to Lie algebras (over ∂_*Id) in T(n)-local spectra.

(5) Algebraic warm-ups. For functors of abelian groups or modules, Eilenberg–MacLane cross-effects (Ann. of Math. 60, 1954, 49–139) make "polynomial degree" precise: F has degree ≤ n iff crₙ₊₁F = 0. A ↦ A⊗A has cr₂(A,B) = (A⊗B) ⊕ (B⊗A) and cr₃ = 0 (degree 2); Symⁿ, Λⁿ, ⊗ⁿ have degree n. Dold–Puppe (Ann. Inst. Fourier 11, 1961, 201–312) derived non-additive functors via simplicial resolutions — their stable derived functors compute stable homology of Eilenberg–MacLane spaces — and Johnson–McCarthy (Trans. AMS 356, 2004, 757–803) built the cotriple Taylor tower interpolating these with Goodwillie's theory. Arone–Ching's Handbook chapter (CRC Press, 2020; arXiv 1902.00803) and their chain rule ∂_*(F∘G) ≃ ∂_*F ∘ ∂_*G (Astérisque 338, 2011) organize the whole subject.

### Key results

- **Derivatives of the identity are dual partition complexes.** For Id: Top* → Top*, there is an equivalence ∂ₙ(Id) ≃ 𝔻(Σ|Πₙ|⋄) of spectra with Σₙ-action, where 𝔻 is Spanier–Whitehead duality. Non-equivariantly ∂ₙ(Id) ≃ ⋁_{(n−1)!} S^{1−n}. Hence Dₙ(X) ≃ Ω^∞((𝔻(Σ|Πₙ|⋄) ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}); P₁Id ≃ Ω^∞Σ^∞, and the tower converges on 1-connected spaces since Id is 1-analytic.  
  *B. Johnson, Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 347 (1995), 1295–1321 (via dual complexes Δₙ); partition-poset reformulation in Arone–Mahowald, Invent. Math. 135 (1999); analyticity of Id in Goodwillie, Calculus II, K-Theory 5 (1991/92), 295–332.*
- **Arone–Mahowald theorem (identity tower on spheres).** Localized at a prime p, the Goodwillie tower of Id evaluated at an odd sphere S^{2m+1} has Dⱼ(S^{2m+1}) contractible unless j = pᵏ; the nontrivial layers are described via the Steinberg/symmetric-power spectra L(k) (partition complex ↔ Tits building comparison), and D_{pᵏ}(S^{2m+1}) has nontrivial vₕ-periodic homotopy only when h = k. Consequently the vₕ-periodic homotopy of odd spheres is captured by the finite stage P_{pʰ} of the tower.  
  *G. Arone and M. Mahowald, Invent. Math. 135 (1999), 743–788; layer identification refined by Arone–Dwyer, Proc. London Math. Soc. 82 (2001), 229–256.*
- **Ching's operad structure on ∂_*(Id).** The simplicial bar construction on a reduced topological/spectral operad is a cooperad; dualizing, the partition-poset models for ∂_*(Id) carry an operad structure making ∂_*(Id) the Koszul dual of the commutative cooperad in spectra. On homology it induces the (shifted) Lie operad: H_{1−n}(∂ₙId) ≅ Lie(n). This is the spectral Lie operad.  
  *M. Ching, Geom. Topol. 9 (2005), 833–934; arXiv math/0501429. Chain rule ∂_*(F∘G) ≃ ∂_*F ∘ ∂_*G: Arone–Ching, Astérisque 338 (2011).*
- **Goodwillie tower and the EHP sequence.** At p = 2 the Goodwillie spectral sequence (from the tower of Id at Sⁿ) and the EHP spectral sequence interlock: transfinite Goodwillie/EHP differentials determine one another through a hierarchy of James–Hopf and suspension maps; the machinery recomputes the 2-primary unstable stems through the Toda range (through the 19-stem) and supports Dyer–Lashof-like operations coming from Ching's operad. Companion result (calculus Whitehead conjecture): the Whitehead sequence of symmetric-power spectra provides a contracting homotopy for the Goodwillie tower of Id at S¹ at p = 2.  
  *M. Behrens, The Goodwillie tower and the EHP sequence, Mem. Amer. Math. Soc. 218 (2012), no. 1026, arXiv 1009.1125; M. Behrens, Algebr. Geom. Topol. 11 (2011), 2453–2475, arXiv 1012.0810 (building on N. Kuhn's proof of the 2-primary Whitehead conjecture).*
- **Taylor tower of Σ^∞Ω^∞ and the Snaith splitting.** For Σ^∞Ω^∞: Sp → Sp, ∂ₙ(Σ^∞Ω^∞) ≃ 𝕊 with trivial Σₙ-action, so Dₙ(X) ≃ (X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}, and Σ^∞Ω^∞X ≃ holimₙ Pₙ(Σ^∞Ω^∞)(X) for 0-connected X. For X = Σ^∞Y with Y a connected based space the tower splits: Σ^∞Ω^∞Σ^∞Y ≃ ⋁_{n≥1} Σ^∞((EΣₙ)₊ ∧_{Σₙ} Y^∧n) — the Snaith splitting, originally proved for Ω^NΣ^N (1 ≤ N ≤ ∞) via May's configuration-space filtration.  
  *V. P. Snaith, J. London Math. Soc. (2) 7 (1974), 577–583; tower formulation standard since Goodwillie, Calculus III, Geom. Topol. 7 (2003), and expounded in Kuhn's survey, Geom. Topol. Monogr. 10 (2007).*
- **Waldhausen: stable A-theory is stable homotopy.** A(X) splits as A(X) ≃ Ω^∞Σ^∞(X₊) × Wh^{Diff}(X), and the stabilization (linearization) of A-theory is stable homotopy: D₁Ã(X) ≃ Σ^∞X. Thus P₁ of A-theory is stable homotopy and everything of higher Taylor degree lives in the smooth Whitehead functor — the starting point for Goodwillie's Calc I analysis of stable pseudoisotopy, which identifies hofib(P(X) → P(Y)) in a range about twice conn(X → Y).  
  *F. Waldhausen, in Algebraic Topology and Algebraic K-theory, Ann. of Math. Stud. 113, Princeton (1987), 392–417; T. Goodwillie, Calculus I, K-Theory 4 (1990), 1–27.*
- **Goodwillie: rational relative K-theory is cyclic homology.** For a map of simplicial rings f: R → S such that π₀(R) → π₀(S) is surjective with nilpotent kernel (a 1-connected map is the special case inducing an isomorphism on π₀), the relative rational K-theory agrees with relative cyclic homology shifted by one: Kₙ(f) ⊗ ℚ ≅ HCₙ₋₁(f) ⊗ ℚ.  
  *T. Goodwillie, Ann. of Math. 124 (1986), 347–402.*
- **Dundas–McCarthy: stable K-theory is THH.** For a ring (or FSP) R with bimodule M, K^S(R;M) ≃ THH(R;M): topological Hochschild homology is the first Goodwillie derivative of K-theory in the bimodule direction. This is the derivative computation underlying all trace-method comparisons of K and TC.  
  *B. I. Dundas and R. McCarthy, Ann. of Math. 140 (1994), 685–701.*
- **Dundas–Goodwillie–McCarthy theorem.** Let A → B be a map of connective ring spectra (or simplicial rings) such that π₀A → π₀B is surjective with nilpotent kernel. Then the cyclotomic-trace square with corners K(A), K(B), TC(A), TC(B) is homotopy cartesian; equivalently relative K-theory ≃ relative TC. In scheme language, K and TC agree relatively along nilpotent immersions. McCarthy proved the profinite statement for nilpotent extensions of simplicial rings, Dundas extended it, and combining with Goodwillie's rational theorem yields the integral statement of the book.  
  *R. McCarthy, Acta Math. 179 (1997), 197–222; B. I. Dundas, Acta Math. 179 (1997), 223–242; B. I. Dundas, T. G. Goodwillie, R. McCarthy, The Local Structure of Algebraic K-Theory, Algebra and Applications 18, Springer, 2013.*
- **Kuhn: T(n)-local Tate vanishing and splitting of Taylor towers.** For n ≥ 1 and any finite group G acting on a T(n)-LOCAL spectrum Z, the Tate construction Z^{tG} is T(n)-acyclic (for arbitrary spectra this is false: by Lin’s theorem S^{tZ/2} is the 2-complete sphere); consequently every Taylor tower of a polynomial functor F: Sp → Sp splits after telescopic localization: L_{T(n)}P_dF(X) ≃ ∏_{j=1}^{d} L_{T(n)}DⱼF(X). Kuhn splitting: for 0-connected X, L_{T(n)}Σ^∞Ω^∞X ≃ ∏_{j≥1} L_{T(n)}(X^∧j)_{hΣⱼ}, naturally in X. The same holds K(n)-locally (Greenlees–Sadofsky vanishing).  
  *N. J. Kuhn, Invent. Math. 157 (2004), 345–370, arXiv math/0307173; applications to K(n)-homology of infinite loop spaces in N. J. Kuhn, Adv. Math. 201 (2006), 318–378, arXiv math/0306098; survey in Geom. Topol. Monogr. 10 (2007), 245–279, arXiv math/0410342.*
- **Heuts: vₙ-periodic spaces are spectral Lie algebras.** The vₙ-periodic homotopy theory of pointed spaces (the localization at vₙ-periodic equivalences, mediated by the Bousfield–Kuhn functor) is equivalent to the homotopy theory of Lie algebras over the spectral Lie operad ∂_*(Id) in T(n)-local spectra, generalizing Quillen's rational (n = 0, Lie algebras over ℚ) theory. The Goodwillie tower of Id provides the comparison.  
  *G. Heuts, Ann. of Math. 193 (2021), 223–301, arXiv 1803.06325.*

### Worked examples suitable for teaching

- Cross-effect warm-up: for F(A) = A ⊗ A on abelian groups, F(A ⊕ B) ≅ (A⊗A) ⊕ (A⊗B) ⊕ (B⊗A) ⊕ (B⊗B), so cr₂F(A,B) = (A⊗B) ⊕ (B⊗A) and cr₃F = 0: F is homogeneous of degree 2. Similarly Sym²(A⊕B) ≅ Sym²A ⊕ (A⊗B) ⊕ Sym²B gives cr₂Sym² = ⊗, and by induction Symⁿ, Λⁿ, ⊗ⁿ all have degree exactly n with crₙ multilinear part the regular-representation-indexed sum of ⊗ⁿ.
- Quadratic functors and stable derived functors: the Dold–Puppe derived functors of Sym² and Λ² on A[n] compute the (co)homology of Eilenberg–MacLane spaces K(A,n) in the metastable range; the stable derived functor (the algebraic linearization / first layer of the Johnson–McCarthy cotriple tower) of Sym² over 𝔽₂ recovers the stable mod-2 homology operations of K(ℤ,n). This is the exact algebraic model of 'P₁ of a quadratic functor'.
- Second layer of the identity at a sphere: ∂₂Id ≃ 𝕊^{−1} with trivial Σ₂-action, so D₂Id(Sⁿ) ≃ Ω^∞Σ^{−1}(Sⁿ ∧ Sⁿ)_{hΣ₂} ≃ Ω^∞Σ^{n−1}Σ^∞(ℝP^∞ₙ), a shifted stunted-projective-space spectrum (Thom space over BΣ₂). The fiber sequence D₂Id(Sⁿ) → P₂Id(Sⁿ) → QSⁿ refines the James/EHP analysis of ΩSⁿ⁺¹; at n = 1 the layer is QℝP^∞, tying the bottom of the S¹-tower to the Kahn–Priddy theorem.
- Counting cells of ∂ₙId: |Π₃| is six points, so ∂₃Id ≃ ⋁₂S^{−2}; |Π₄| ≃ ⋁₆S¹, so ∂₄Id ≃ ⋁₆S^{−3}. In general H^{n−1}(Σ|Πₙ|⋄) is free of rank (n−1)! and carries the Lie(n) representation of Σₙ (up to sign twist) — students can verify n = 3 by hand from the poset.
- Snaith splitting in practice: for Y = S¹, Σ^∞QS¹ ≃ ⋁ₙ Σ^∞(S¹)^{∧n}_{hΣₙ} with (S¹∧S¹)_{hΣ₂} ≃ ΣℝP^∞ in the n = 2 slot; the mod-p homology of the summands assembles the free Dyer–Lashof algebra on one generator — the tower of Σ^∞Ω^∞ 'is' the homology suspension filtration.
- Trace methods on dual numbers: for the square-zero extension 𝔽ₚ[x]/x² → 𝔽ₚ the DGM theorem gives K(𝔽ₚ[x]/x², (x)) ≃ TC(𝔽ₚ[x]/x², (x); p), which Hesselholt–Madsen computed: K_{2i−1}(𝔽ₚ[x]/x², (x)) is cyclic of order pⁱ (i ≥ 1, up to standard renormalization) and even groups vanish — a complete calculation unreachable by direct K-theory methods.
- Kuhn splitting numerically: since Tate spectra of finite groups vanish T(n)-locally, the k-invariants of the Σ^∞Ω^∞-tower (which factor through norm/Tate obstructions) die after L_{T(n)}; e.g. Morava K-theory of Ω^∞X for 0-connected X is computed from ∏ⱼ K(n)_*((X^∧j)_{hΣⱼ}) — the starting point of Kuhn's Adv. Math. 2006 computations.
- Rational sanity check: rationally |Πₙ|-duality gives H_*(∂ₙId; ℚ) = Lie(n) in degree 1−n, and the tower of Id on a simply connected rational space degenerates to Quillen's Lie-algebra model of rational homotopy theory — the n = 0 case of Heuts' theorem, good for calibrating conventions.

### Caveats and misconception traps

- Convergence is a hypothesis, not a formality: the tower of Id converges on simply connected (more generally nilpotent) spaces because Id is 1-analytic; it fails to converge on general spaces. The tower of Σ^∞Ω^∞ needs 0-connected spectra. Never write X ≃ holim PₙId(X) without a connectivity assumption.
- Kuhn's splitting theorem splits the T(n)-localized tower; it does not by itself assert L_{T(n)}Σ^∞Ω^∞X ≃ ∏ⱼL_{T(n)}(X^∧j)_{hΣⱼ} for arbitrary X — the identification of the limit still uses convergence (0-connectivity). Splitting of a tower and convergence of a tower are independent issues.
- T(n) vs K(n): Kuhn's results are stated for the telescopic localizations T(n) (and hold K(n)-locally too, cf. Greenlees–Sadofsky). Since the 2023 disproof of the telescope conjecture (Burklund–Hahn–Levy–Schlank), T(n) ≠ K(n) for n ≥ 2, so the two statements are genuinely different; do not silently interchange them.
- The DGM theorem is relative: K and TC agree on relative terms along maps with π₀-surjective, nilpotent kernel (nilpotent immersions). Absolutely, K ≠ TC. Also mind the historical layering: McCarthy 1997 is profinite for simplicial rings, Goodwillie 1986 is rational, and the integral connective-ring-spectrum statement is the 2013 book's.
- The full Taylor tower of A-theory is not known; what is proved are the linearization (Waldhausen: stable A-theory ≃ stable homotopy), Calc I's stable-range identification for pseudoisotopy, and the trace-method identifications of the derivative (THH). A 2014 preprint computing the first differential of A(X) at a general base (arXiv 1406.3551, Ullmann) was withdrawn due to an error — do not cite it.
- Equivariance pitfall: ∂ₙId ≃ ⋁_{(n−1)!}S^{1−n} holds only non-equivariantly; the Σₙ-equivariant homotopy type (needed for the layers) is 𝔻(Σ|Πₙ|⋄), and homotopy orbits (X^∧n)_{hΣₙ} — not fixed points, not naive quotients — play the role of dividing by n!.
- Grading/suspension conventions for the spectral Lie operad differ across sources: H_*(∂ₙId) is Lie(n) placed in degree 1 − n, often written as a suspension/sign twist of the classical Lie operad; check each paper's convention before comparing formulas.
- The Whitehead conjecture (symmetric-power filtration of H𝕫... i.e. of the sphere via Sp^{pᵏ}) is a theorem — Kuhn proved the 2-primary case, Kuhn–Priddy the odd-primary case — and Behrens (2011) proved its 'calculus form' for the S¹-tower at p = 2. Do not present it as open.
- Arone–Mahowald's vanishing (layers only at j = pᵏ) is a p-local statement about odd-dimensional spheres; even spheres and integral statements require extra care (EHP-style comparisons, Behrens 2012).
- Bibliographic quirk: Goodwillie's Calculus II appears in K-Theory vol. 5 with cover dates 1991/92 — both '1991' and '1992' occur in the literature; the KnotLab references.json entry should pick one convention and note the other.

- Taylor-tower stack widget: a functor picker (Id, Σ^∞Ω^∞, Sym², A-theory) draws the tower with clickable stages PₙF and layers DₙF; hovering a layer shows the formula Ω^∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}) with the specific ∂ₙF substituted, and a convergence badge showing the required connectivity (ρ = 1 for Id, ρ = 0 for Σ^∞Ω^∞).
- Symbolic cross-effect calculator: user enters an algebraic functor built from ⊗, Sym, Λ; the app expands F(A₁ ⊕ … ⊕ Aₖ) using bi-additivity rules, colors the multilinear summand, reports crₖF, and concludes the polynomial degree — directly implementing Eilenberg–MacLane (1954).
- Stunted projective space demo: slider n displays D₂Id(Sⁿ) ≃ Ω^∞Σ^{n−1}Σ^∞ℝP^∞ₙ with its mod-2 homology ladder and Steenrod action, illustrating how the quadratic layer of the identity sees Whitehead products and the EHP sequence; n = 1 links to Kahn–Priddy.
- Chromatic heat map: a grid indexed by layer pᵏ (rows) and chromatic height h (columns) for the tower of Id at S^{2m+1}, shading cells where vₕ-periodic homotopy of D_{pᵏ} is nonzero — a single diagonal, visualizing the Arone–Mahowald theorem; a toggle applies L_{T(n)} and animates Kuhn's splitting of the Σ^∞Ω^∞ tower (k-invariants labeled by Tate spectra fade out).
- Trace-methods sandbox: table comparing K_*(𝔽ₚ[x]/x², (x)) with the TC computation in low degrees, plus a schematic of the DGM cartesian square with the nilpotence hypothesis highlighted; exercise asks why the theorem fails without nilpotence (absolute K vs TC of 𝔽ₚ).
- Exercise set (algebraic warm-up): (a) show cr₃(A ↦ A⊗A) = 0 and compute cr₂; (b) compute the cotriple tower of Sym² in chain complexes over 𝔽₂ through degree 2 and compare with H_*(K(ℤ, n); 𝔽₂) in the stable range; (c) verify that additive functors are exactly the degree-1 ones.
- Snaith splitting explorer: for user-chosen connected Y (S¹, S², ℝP²), list the first four stable summands (Y^∧j)_{hΣⱼ} of Σ^∞QY with their integral homology computed via extended powers; emphasize that the splitting is stable and non-canonical (James–Hopf choices).
- Dictionary quiz: drag-and-drop matching of ordinary-calculus concepts (Taylor coefficient, radius of convergence, chain rule, f′ = g′ ⇒ f − g constant) to their functor-calculus counterparts, with the DGM theorem as the punchline of the last one.

## 4. Manifold calculus and spaces of knots

Manifold calculus — also called embedding calculus or Goodwillie–Weiss calculus — approximates contravariant isotopy functors on a smooth manifold M, i.e. presheaves F: O(M)ᵒᵖ → Top on the poset O(M) of open subsets, by "polynomial" ones. It was introduced by Weiss in "Embeddings from the point of view of immersion theory: Part I" (Geom. Topol. 3 (1999) 67–101; erratum Geom. Topol. 15 (2011) 407–409) and Goodwillie–Weiss, "…Part II" (Geom. Topol. 3 (1999) 103–118). F is polynomial of degree ≤ k if for every open V and pairwise disjoint nonempty closed A₀,…,A_k ⊆ V, the (k+1)-cube S ↦ F(V ∖ ⋃_{i∈S}Aᵢ) is homotopy cartesian. The k-th Taylor approximation is T_kF(V) = holim_{U∈O_k(V)} F(U), where O_k(V) consists of opens diffeomorphic to ≤ k disjoint balls: a degree ≤ k presheaf is determined by its values on ≤ k balls, just as a degree ≤ k polynomial is determined by k+1 values. Boavida de Brito–Weiss (Homology Homotopy Appl. 15 (2013) 361–383) recast T_k as homotopy sheafification. The stages assemble into the Taylor tower F → ⋯ → T_kF → T_{k−1}F → ⋯ → T₁F. For F = Emb(−,N) the linear stage is immersion theory: T₁Emb(−,N) ≃ Imm(−,N) by the Smale–Hirsch h-principle — hence Weiss's title. The k-th layer L_kF = hofib(T_kF → T_{k−1}F) is a compactly-supported section space over the space of unordered k-point configurations in M — the "k-th derivative".

Convergence is the deep theorem. Goodwillie–Weiss (1999) formulated, and the multirelative disjunction results of Goodwillie–Klein, "Multiple disjunction for spaces of smooth embeddings" (J. Topol. 8 (2015) 651–674) completed the proof, that if M has handle dimension m and n = dim N with n − m ≥ 3, then Emb(M,N) → T_kEmb(M,N) is (k(n−m−2)+1−m)-connected. Since the connectivity grows with k precisely when n−m−2 > 0, the tower converges — Emb(M,N) ≃ holim_k T_kEmb(M,N) — in handle codimension ≥ 3.

For knot theory the functor is long knots, Emb_c(ℝ,ℝ^d): embeddings agreeing with a fixed line outside a compact set (π₀ for d = 3 is classical knot theory). Here m = 1 and the map Emb_c → T_k is k(d−3)-connected: for d ≥ 4 the tower converges, while for d = 3 (codimension 2) the estimate is vacuous and convergence is a fundamental open problem. Sinha made the tower computable. "The topology of spaces of knots: cosimplicial models" (Amer. J. Math. 131 (2009) 945–980) gives two small models for T_n — a punctured-knots homotopy limit over the poset of nonempty S ⊆ {0,…,n} (arcs with |S| punctures) and a mapping space model of aligned maps between compactified configuration spaces — weakly equivalent to the knot space when d ≥ 4. "Operads and knot spaces" (J. Amer. Math. Soc. 19 (2006) 461–486) shows the Kontsevich operad K_d, an operad with multiplication, has cosimplicial space K_d• with Tot ⁿK_d• ≃ T_nEmb̄_c(ℝ,ℝ^d), where Emb̄ = hofib(Emb_c → Imm_c) is "long knots modulo immersions" (Sinha: Emb̄ ≃ Emb_c × Ω²S^{d−1}, since Imm_c(ℝ,ℝ^d) ≃ ΩS^{d−1}); McClure–Smith machinery then gives a little-2-cubes action on Emb̄. The associated Bousfield–Kan spectral sequences are the calculus incarnation of Vassiliev's spectral sequence. Boavida de Brito–Weiss (J. Topol. 11 (2018) 65–143) reformulated the whole tower via configuration categories.

Classical knots, d = 3. Budney–Conant–Scannell–Sinha, "New perspectives on self-linking" (Adv. Math. 191 (2005) 78–113) computed the homotopy types of the first three mapping space models and showed the third carries a ℤ-valued invariant equal to c₂, the second Conway coefficient — the simplest type-2 invariant — realized by counting collinearity data (quadrisecants). Volić (Compos. Math. 142 (2006) 222–250) showed that over ℝ every type-n invariant factors through T_{2n}, making the tower a universal real finite-type invariant. Budney–Conant–Koytcheff–Sinha, "Embedding calculus knot invariants are of finite type" (Algebr. Geom. Topol. 17 (2017) 1701–1742) proved π₀T_nEmb_c(ℝ,ℝ³) is an abelian group and ev_n: π₀Emb_c → π₀T_n is a monoid map invariant under degree-n clasper surgery, hence a type-(n−1) invariant; they computed the E² page of the homotopy spectral sequence in total degree 0 as ℤ-modules of primitive chord diagrams, and conjectured ev_{n+1} is a universal additive type-n invariant over ℤ. Status: Kosanović (Adv. Math. 451 (2024), art. 109779) proved ev_n is surjective for long knots in any 3-manifold via grope cobordism, identifying the first possibly nonvanishing ev_n of a knot grope-cobordant to the unknot with the class of the grope's underlying decorated tree in a graph complex; Boavida de Brito–Horel, "Galois symmetries of knot spaces" (Compos. Math. 157 (2021) 997–1021) used Galois symmetries of profinite little-disks operads to kill differentials p-locally, showing the (n+1)-st approximation is a p-local universal Vassiliev invariant of degree ≤ n for n ≤ p+1. Combined, ev_n is a universal additive Vassiliev invariant rationally; over ℤ universality remains a conjecture.

Rational homology in high codimension: Lambrechts–Turchin–Volić, "The rational homology of spaces of long knots in codimension > 2" (Geom. Topol. 14 (2010) 2151–2187) prove that for d ≥ 4 the Vassiliev (Sinha) homology spectral sequence collapses rationally at E¹, using relative Kontsevich formality of the little d-disks operad; hence H⁎(Emb̄_c(ℝ,ℝ^d); ℚ) is the Hochschild homology of the degree-(d−1) Poisson operad, computable from a graph complex. Integrally and for d = 3 this fails: Marino–Salvatore (arXiv 2504.16785, preprint 2025) exhibit a nonzero d₃ in the mod-2 Sinha spectral sequence for knots in ℝ³. Finally, operad structure organizes the knot space itself: Budney, "Little cubes and long knots" (Topology 46 (2007) 1–27) shows the 2-cubes action extending connected sum makes K = Emb_c(ℝ,ℝ³) a FREE little-2-cubes algebra, K ≃ C₂(P ⊔ {∗}) with P the prime long knots — a space-level Schubert theorem tied to JSJ decompositions of knot complements.

### Key results

- **Polynomial approximation and classification of homogeneous functors.** For a good cofunctor F on O(M), T_kF(V) = holim_{U∈O_k(V)}F(U) is polynomial of degree ≤ k and F → T_kF is universal among maps to degree ≤ k cofunctors; homogeneous degree-k cofunctors are equivalent to compactly-supported section spaces of fibrations over the unordered k-point configuration space of M. For F = Emb(−,N), T₁Emb ≃ Imm (Smale–Hirsch h-principle).  
  *M. Weiss, "Embeddings from the point of view of immersion theory: Part I", Geom. Topol. 3 (1999) 67–101 (erratum: Geom. Topol. 15 (2011) 407–409)*
- **Analyticity of the embedding functor (codimension ≥ 3).** The cofunctor V ↦ Emb(V,N) on an m-manifold satisfies higher excision estimates (is analytic) when dim N − m ≥ 3, and consequently its Taylor tower converges to it: Emb(M,N) ≃ holim_k T_kEmb(M,N) in handle codimension ≥ 3.  
  *T. G. Goodwillie and M. Weiss, "Embeddings from the point of view of immersion theory: Part II", Geom. Topol. 3 (1999) 103–118 (relying on disjunction results proved in Goodwillie–Klein 2015)*
- **Goodwillie–Klein convergence / connectivity estimate.** Let M have handle dimension m and n = dim N with n − m ≥ 3. Then Emb(M,N) → T_kEmb(M,N) is (k(n−m−2)+1−m)-connected for every k ≥ 1. For long knots (m = 1) the map Emb_c(ℝ,ℝ^d) → T_k is k(d−3)-connected: convergence for d ≥ 4, no information for d = 3.  
  *T. G. Goodwillie and J. R. Klein, "Multiple disjunction for spaces of smooth embeddings", J. Topol. 8 (2015), no. 3, 651–674 (announced with Goodwillie–Weiss 1999)*
- **Cosimplicial and mapping-space models for the knot tower.** There are explicit models for T_nEmb: (i) the punctured-knots holim over nonempty S ⊆ {0,…,n}; (ii) a mapping space model of aligned maps between compactified configuration spaces; both weakly equivalent to the space of knots when the ambient dimension is ≥ 4. Moreover Totⁿ of the Kontsevich operad's cosimplicial space computes T_nEmb̄_c(ℝ,ℝ^d), Tot ≃ Emb̄_c(ℝ,ℝ^d) for d ≥ 4, and McClure–Smith theory gives a little-2-cubes action on Emb̄.  
  *D. Sinha, "The topology of spaces of knots: cosimplicial models", Amer. J. Math. 131 (2009), no. 4, 945–980; and "Operads and knot spaces", J. Amer. Math. Soc. 19 (2006), no. 2, 461–486*
- **First stages and c₂ via quadrisecants.** The homotopy types of the first three mapping space models for classical long knots are computed; the third model carries a ℤ-valued isotopy invariant equal to c₂, the second coefficient of the Conway polynomial (the unique type-2 invariant with v(unknot)=0, v(trefoil)=1), realized geometrically by counting collinearities of 3 and 4 points on the knot (quadrisecants).  
  *R. Budney, J. Conant, K. Scannell, D. Sinha, "New perspectives on self-linking", Adv. Math. 191 (2005), no. 1, 78–113*
- **Real universality through even stages.** Over ℝ, every type-n Vassiliev invariant of long knots in ℝ³ factors through the stage T_{2n} of the Taylor tower; along total degree 0 the cohomology spectral sequence collapses and reproduces the chord-diagram combinatorics of finite-type theory, so the tower is a universal real finite-type invariant.  
  *I. Volić, "Finite type knot invariants and the calculus of functors", Compos. Math. 142 (2006) 222–250*
- **Embedding calculus knot invariants are of finite type.** For classical long knots, π₀T_nEmb_c(ℝ,ℝ³) is an abelian group; ev_n: π₀Emb_c(ℝ,ℝ³) → π₀T_n is a monoid homomorphism invariant under degree-n clasper (grope) surgery, hence a finite-type invariant of degree ≤ n−1. The E² page of the homotopy spectral sequence in total degree 0 is isomorphic to ℤ-modules of primitive chord diagrams. Conjecture (BCKS): ev_{n+1} is a universal additive Vassiliev invariant of degree ≤ n over ℤ.  
  *R. Budney, J. Conant, R. Koytcheff, D. Sinha, "Embedding calculus knot invariants are of finite type", Algebr. Geom. Topol. 17 (2017), no. 3, 1701–1742*
- **Galois symmetries and p-local universality.** Using Galois symmetries of profinite completions of the little-disks operads, many differentials of the Goodwillie–Weiss homology and homotopy spectral sequences for knot spaces vanish p-locally; consequently the (n+1)-st Goodwillie–Weiss approximation is a p-local universal Vassiliev invariant of degree ≤ n for every n ≤ p+1.  
  *P. Boavida de Brito and G. Horel, "Galois symmetries of knot spaces", Compos. Math. 157 (2021), no. 5, 997–1021*
- **Surjectivity of ev_n and rational universality.** For long knots in any 3-manifold, ev_n: π₀Emb → π₀T_n is surjective; the first possibly nonvanishing ev_n of a knot grope-cobordant to the unknot equals the class of the grope's underlying decorated tree in the associated graph complex. Combined with Boavida de Brito–Horel, the ev_n are universal additive Vassiliev invariants over ℚ (and over p-adic integers in a range), confirming the surjectivity half of the BCKS conjecture over ℤ.  
  *D. Kosanović, "Embedding calculus and grope cobordism of knots", Adv. Math. 451 (2024), Paper No. 109779*
- **Rational collapse in codimension > 2.** For d ≥ 4, the Vassiliev (Sinha / Bousfield–Kan) spectral sequence computing H⁎(Emb̄_c(ℝ,ℝ^d); ℚ) collapses at the E¹ page; hence the rational homology of long knots modulo immersions is the Hochschild homology of the Poisson operad with bracket of degree d−1, computable from an explicit graph complex. The proof combines a relative version of Kontsevich's formality of the little d-disks operad with Sinha's cosimplicial model.  
  *P. Lambrechts, V. Turchin, I. Volić, "The rational homology of spaces of long knots in codimension > 2", Geom. Topol. 14 (2010), no. 4, 2151–2187*
- **Budney's freeness theorem.** The space K of long knots in ℝ³ admits a little-2-cubes action extending the connected-sum monoid structure, and K ≃ C₂(P ⊔ {∗}), the free little-2-cubes algebra on the subspace P of prime long knots (with disjoint basepoint). The proof reveals a close correspondence between the JSJ decomposition of knot complements and the cubes action — a space-level refinement of Schubert's prime decomposition.  
  *R. Budney, "Little cubes and long knots", Topology 46 (2007), no. 1, 1–27*
- **Manifold calculus as homotopy sheafification; configuration categories.** T_k is homotopy sheafification with respect to the Grothendieck topology of covers by ≤ k balls; and the embedding tower can be described via derived mapping spaces of configuration categories, yielding an operadic description of the obstructions to deforming smooth immersions into embeddings.  
  *P. Boavida de Brito and M. Weiss, "Manifold calculus and homotopy sheaves", Homology Homotopy Appl. 15 (2013), no. 2, 361–383; and "Spaces of smooth embeddings and configuration categories", J. Topol. 11 (2018), no. 1, 65–143*

### Worked examples suitable for teaching

- Connectivity arithmetic for long knots (m = 1): the map Emb_c(ℝ,ℝ^d) → T_kEmb_c(ℝ,ℝ^d) is k(d−3)-connected. Table: d = 3 ⇒ 0-connected for ALL k (the theorem says nothing); d = 4 ⇒ k-connected (T₅ already computes π≤₄); d = 5 ⇒ 2k-connected. This one computation explains why classical knot theory is the hard boundary case.
- The linear stage sees no knots: T₁Emb_c(ℝ,ℝ³) ≃ Imm_c(ℝ,ℝ³) ≃ ΩS² by Smale–Hirsch, and π₀ΩS² = π₁(S²) = 0: every long knot can be undone through immersions (regular homotopy). Knotting is invisible to immersion theory and to the early tower stages; the first π₀-information appears at the third stage.
- c₂ from the third stage (BCSS): ev₃ lands in π₀T₃ ≅ ℤ and equals the second Conway coefficient, computable from quadrisecant/collinearity counts. Check against the Conway polynomial: ∇(unknot) = 1 ⇒ c₂ = 0; ∇(3₁) = 1 + z² ⇒ c₂ = 1 (both chiralities — an even-type invariant cannot detect mirrors); ∇(4₁) = 1 − z² ⇒ c₂ = −1. These match KnotLab's Conway data and give a purely geometric definition of the simplest Vassiliev invariant.
- Punctured knots are configuration spaces: a long arc with k open subintervals removed retracts to the data of k points with unit tangent vectors, so Emb of ≤ n punctured long knots is equivalent to compactified configuration-space data, and T_n is the finite homotopy limit of these spaces over the poset of nonempty S ⊆ {0,…,n} — a completely explicit "sampling at ≤ n points" approximation.
- Space-level Schubert theorem: π₀Emb_c(ℝ,ℝ³) is the free commutative monoid on prime knots (Schubert 1949). Budney's K ≃ C₂(P ⊔ {∗}) lifts this to the space level: connected sum is the little-2-cubes multiplication and the algebra is free on prime long knots. Combined with Hatcher's theorem that the unknot component is contractible, this organizes the entire homotopy type of the space of classical long knots.
- Coefficients matter in the Vassiliev spectral sequence: over ℚ with d ≥ 4 it collapses at E¹ (Lambrechts–Turchin–Volić 2010), so H⁎(Emb̄_c(ℝ,ℝ^d);ℚ) = Hochschild homology of the Poisson_{d−1} operad, computed by a graph complex; but mod 2 for d = 3 there is a nonzero d₃ differential out of a 2-dimensional class (Marino–Salvatore 2025 preprint, computer-assisted) — a concrete failure of collapse outside the rational, high-codimension regime.

### Caveats and misconception traps

- Convergence is a theorem ONLY in handle codimension ≥ 3 (dim N − handle dim M ≥ 3). For classical knots (codimension 2) the estimate k(d−3) = 0 is vacuous: it is NOT known whether Emb_c(ℝ,ℝ³) → T_∞ is an equivalence, but non-convergence has not been proven either — state neither as fact.
- Universality status must be kept straight: "ev_{n+1} is a universal additive type-n invariant" is a THEOREM over ℚ (Kosanović 2024 + Boavida de Brito–Horel 2021), a THEOREM ℤ₍p₎-locally for n ≤ p+1 (Boavida de Brito–Horel 2021), and a CONJECTURE over ℤ (BCKS 2017). Kosanović's surjectivity is exactly one half of the ℤ conjecture.
- Indexing off-by-one hazards: the n-th tower stage T_n gives invariants of type ≤ n−1 (BCKS); the "third mapping space model" of BCSS corresponds to the third stage and yields the type-2 invariant c₂; Volić's real-coefficient result needs the 2n-th stage T_{2n} for type n. Different papers index by number of configuration points vs invariant degree — fix one convention in the module and say so.
- Citation traps: the BCSS paper is "New perspectives on self-linking" (Adv. Math. 191 (2005) 78–113) — not "self-intersection", and not in AGT; the 2017 author list is Budney–Conant–Koytcheff–Sinha (Koytcheff, not Scannell); Goodwillie–Klein appeared in J. Topol. 8 (2015), sixteen years after the convergence theorem was announced in Goodwillie–Weiss Part II (1999) — attribute the theorem to all of Goodwillie, Klein, Weiss with both dates.
- Emb vs Emb̄: Sinha's operad/cosimplicial models and the LTV computation concern Emb̄_c(ℝ,ℝ^d) = hofib(Emb_c → Imm_c), long knots MODULO IMMERSIONS. The relation is Emb̄ ≃ Emb_c × Ω²S^{d−1} (Sinha), with Imm_c(ℝ,ℝ^d) ≃ ΩS^{d−1} by Smale–Hirsch. Conflating Emb and Emb̄ silently changes homology and homotopy groups.
- The LTV collapse theorem is rational and requires d ≥ 4 ("codimension > 2" means codimension ≥ 3 for 1-dimensional sources). It does not extend to d = 3 or to torsion coefficients: Marino–Salvatore (arXiv 2504.16785) find a nonzero mod-2 d₃ for d = 3 — but that is a 2025 PREPRINT; flag its status.
- Weiss's Part I has a published erratum (Geom. Topol. 15 (2011) 407–409) repairing the proof that T_kF is polynomial; cite the corrected statement.
- T₁ ≃ Imm uses the Smale–Hirsch h-principle, which requires positive handle codimension (no closed component of dimension = dim N); it holds for long knots. Note π₁(S²) = 0: immersion theory sees no knotting, so all π₀-information enters at higher stages, first nontrivially at the third stage via c₂.
- "Additive" matters: ev_n are monoid homomorphisms on (π₀Emb_c, #), so the universality conjecture concerns additive (primitive) Vassiliev invariants; general finite-type invariants are polynomials in additive ones.
- Claims that embedding calculus invariants are "strictly stronger than the Kontsevich integral" overstate current theorems: what is known is that ev_n are defined over ℤ, are universal over ℚ (hence dominate the Kontsevich integral rationally), and are conjectured to see torsion the Kontsevich integral misses.

## 5. Other flavors of functor calculus and unifying frameworks

Goodwillie's homotopy calculus is one member of a family of "calculi of functors," each adapted to a different kind of input, and the past two decades have produced genuine unifying frameworks. This review covers the orthogonal, discrete/abelian, operadic, ∞-categorical, and tangent-categorical branches.

(1) Orthogonal calculus. Michael Weiss ("Orthogonal calculus", Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 347 (1995), no. 10, 3743–3796; Erratum, Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 350 (1998), 851–855) developed a calculus for continuous functors E: J → Top from the topological category J of finite-dimensional real inner-product spaces and linear isometric embeddings. E is polynomial of degree ≤ n if for all V the map E(V) → holim over nonzero subspaces 0 ≠ U ⊆ ℝⁿ⁺¹ of E(U ⊕ V) is a weak equivalence. Weiss constructs a tower of universal polynomial approximations TₙE and classifies the homogeneous layers: an n-homogeneous functor has the form V ↦ Ω^∞((S^(ℝⁿ⊗V) ∧ Θ)_hO(n)) for a spectrum Θ = Θ_E⁽ⁿ⁾ with O(n)-action — the n-th derivative. So where Goodwillie calculus has Σₙ-spectra, orthogonal calculus has O(n)-spectra. Weiss's motivating examples are V ↦ BO(V), V ↦ BU(V), and V ↦ BTop(V); his abstract states that first derivatives reproduce and generalize much of Stiefel–Whitney theory and second derivatives much of Pontryagin theory. The first derivative of BO(−) is the sphere spectrum 𝕊, via the loop–suspension comparison S^d = O(d+1)/O(d) → Ω(O(d+2)/O(d+1)) = ΩS^(d+1). Arone ("The Weiss derivatives of BO(−) and BU(−)", Topology 41 (2002), 451–481) identified all the derivatives of BO(−) and BU(−) and computed the homology of the layers; Krannich–Randal-Williams (arXiv:2109.03500) computed the second rational Weiss derivative of BTop(−), with dramatic applications to Diff_∂(D^d). Model-categorical foundations are due to Barnes–Oman ("Model categories for orthogonal calculus", Algebr. Geom. Topol. 13 (2013), 959–999), including a zig-zag of Quillen equivalences refining Weiss's classification; Barnes–Eldred ("Comparing the orthogonal and homotopy functor calculi", J. Pure Appl. Algebra 220 (2016), 3650–3675) show the orthogonal and Goodwillie towers agree up to weak equivalence on analytic input.

(2) Discrete/abelian calculus. The roots are Eilenberg–Mac Lane's cross effects ("On the groups H(Π,n). II", Ann. of Math. (2) 60 (1954), 49–139) and Dold–Puppe's derived functors of non-additive functors ("Homologie nicht-additiver Funktoren. Anwendungen", Ann. Inst. Fourier 11 (1961), 201–312). Johnson–McCarthy ("Linearization, Dold–Puppe stabilization, and Mac Lane's Q-construction", Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 350 (1998), 1555–1593) proved that Goodwillie-style linearization, Dold–Puppe stabilization, and Mac Lane's Q-construction produce equivalent linear approximations under natural hypotheses. In "Deriving calculus with cotriples" (Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 356 (2004), no. 2, 757–803) they built a Taylor tower for functors from a pointed category to an abelian (or chain-complex) category: the (n+1)-st cross effect sits in an adjunction whose associated cotriple ⊥ₙ₊₁ acts on functors, and PₙF is the cofiber of the augmented cotriple resolution |⊥ₙ₊₁^(•+1)F| → F; here "degree ≤ n" means crₙ₊₁F ≃ 0. Bauer–Johnson–McCarthy, with an appendix by Eldred ("Cross effects and calculus in an unbased setting", Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 367 (2015), no. 9, 6671–6718; arXiv:1101.1025), extended cross effects and the cotriple ("discrete") tower to functors from a category C_f of objects factoring a fixed map f: A → B to spectra, and compared the discrete degree-n tower with Goodwillie's n-excisive tower: n-excisive implies degree n, and the towers are related by explicit comparison maps. Bauer–Johnson–Osborne–Riehl–Tebbe ("Directional derivatives and higher order chain rules for abelian functor calculus", Topology Appl. 235 (2018), 375–427) showed abelian functor calculus carries a directional derivative making a suitable homotopy category of abelian categories a cartesian differential category in the sense of Blute–Cockett–Seely, with a Faà di Bruno-type higher-order chain rule — a first bridge to the differential-categories literature.

(3) Chain rules and operads. Klein–Rognes ("A chain rule in the calculus of homotopy functors", Geom. Topol. 6 (2002), 853–887) proved the first-derivative chain rule for composites of homotopy functors of spaces. Ching ("Bar constructions for topological operads and the Goodwillie derivatives of the identity", Geom. Topol. 9 (2005), 833–934) put an operad structure on the partition-poset models of ∂_*Id_{S_*} whose homology is the (shifted) Lie operad — the spectral Lie operad. Ching then proved the full chain rule for functors of spectra: ∂_*(F∘G) at X is the composition product of ∂_*F at G(X) with ∂_*G at X ("A chain rule for Goodwillie derivatives of functors from spectra to spectra", Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 362 (2010), 399–426). Arone–Ching ("Operads and chain rules for the calculus of functors", Astérisque 338 (2011), vi+158 pp.; arXiv:0902.0399) showed that for reduced finitary homotopy functors among based spaces and spectra, ∂_*F is naturally a bimodule over the operad ∂_*Id, and ∂_*(F∘G) ≃ ∂_*F ∘_{∂_*Id} ∂_*G, a derived composition product over ∂_*Id, extending Klein–Rognes to all derivatives.

(4) ∞-categorical Goodwillie calculus. Lurie's Higher Algebra (2017 preprint), Chapter 6 "The Calculus of Functors", redevelops the theory for functors F: C → D with C admitting finite colimits and a final object and D differentiable (finite limits + sequential colimits commuting with them). F is n-excisive if it sends strongly coCartesian (n+1)-cubes to Cartesian cubes; Theorem 6.1.1.10 (Goodwillie's theorem, ∞-categorically) produces the universal n-excisive approximation PₙF as a localization, hence the Taylor tower. Cross effects and the classification of n-homogeneous functors by symmetric multilinear functors occupy §6.1.3–6.1.4; §6.2 treats differentiation and §6.3 the chain rule. Crucially, stabilization is defined by Sp(C) := Exc(S^fin_∗, C) — reduced excisive functors from finite pointed spaces — so Sp = Sp(S_∗): spectra literally are the reduced, finitary 1-excisive functors S_∗ → S_∗, making stable ∞-categories the "linear part" of homotopy theory.

(5) The standard modern survey is Arone–Ching, "Goodwillie calculus", Chapter 1 of the Handbook of Homotopy Theory (ed. H. Miller, CRC Press/Chapman & Hall, 2020; arXiv:1902.00803), covering towers, derivatives, classification of Taylor towers, chain rules, and the other calculi.

(6) Tangent ∞-categories. Bauer–Burke–Ching ("Tangent ∞-categories and Goodwillie calculus", arXiv:2101.07819; still a preprint as of mid-2026, v5 June 2026) make the calculus/geometry analogy precise: they extend Cockett–Cruttwell tangent categories (originally Rosický, 1984) to ∞-categories and prove that Lurie's tangent bundle construction — TC with fibers T_XC ≃ Sp(C_{/X}), modeled by excisive functors — is the tangent bundle of a "Goodwillie tangent structure" on the ∞-category of differentiable ∞-categories. In this dictionary stable ∞-categories play the role of Euclidean spaces, and n-excisive functors are the direct analogues of n-jets. Together with BJORT's cartesian differential category and the discrete calculus, this places all the flavors inside one axiomatic differential-geometric framework.

### Key results

- **Orthogonal Taylor tower and classification of homogeneous functors.** For continuous E: J → Top there is a tower of universal polynomial approximations E → TₙE → Tₙ₋₁E → …; a functor is n-homogeneous (degree ≤ n with trivial Tₙ₋₁) iff it is equivalent to V ↦ Ω^∞((S^(ℝⁿ⊗V) ∧ Θ)_hO(n)) for a spectrum Θ with O(n)-action, unique up to equivalence. First derivatives reproduce and generalize Stiefel–Whitney theory; second derivatives Pontryagin theory.  
  *M. Weiss, Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 347 (1995), no. 10, 3743–3796; Erratum, Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 350 (1998), 851–855*
- **Model categories for orthogonal calculus.** The n-homogeneous functors J → Top form a model category zig-zag Quillen equivalent to spectra with O(n)-action, upgrading Weiss's classification to an equivalence of homotopy theories.  
  *D. Barnes, P. Oman, Algebr. Geom. Topol. 13 (2013), 959–999 (arXiv:1101.4099)*
- **Agreement of linearizations.** For functors of (simplicial) modules/chain complexes, Goodwillie-style linearization D₁, Dold–Puppe stabilization, and Mac Lane's Q-construction produce naturally equivalent linear approximations under suitable hypotheses; in particular D₁ recovers the stable derived functors of Dold–Puppe.  
  *B. Johnson, R. McCarthy, Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 350 (1998), no. 4, 1555–1593*
- **Cotriple Taylor tower (discrete/abelian calculus).** For F from a pointed category to an abelian (or chain-complex) category, the cotriples ⊥ₙ₊₁ arising from cross-effect adjunctions produce a tower F → … → PₙF → Pₙ₋₁F → … with PₙF of degree ≤ n (i.e. crₙ₊₁PₙF ≃ 0) and universal among degree-≤n functors under F, up to natural weak equivalence.  
  *B. Johnson, R. McCarthy, "Deriving calculus with cotriples", Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 356 (2004), no. 2, 757–803*
- **Cross effects and discrete calculus in an unbased setting.** For F: C_f → S (C_f = objects factoring a fixed f: A → B in a simplicial model category, S spectra), cross effects are defined via explicit adjoint pairs whose cotriples are diagonals of the cross effects; the resulting discrete tower has degree-n terms, every n-excisive functor is degree n, and comparison maps relate the discrete tower to Goodwillie's tower.  
  *K. Bauer, B. Johnson, R. McCarthy (appendix by R. Eldred), Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 367 (2015), no. 9, 6671–6718 (arXiv:1101.1025)*
- **Abelian functor calculus is a cartesian differential category.** A homotopy category of abelian categories and chain-complex-valued functors, equipped with the directional derivative built from linearization of cross effects, satisfies the Blute–Cockett–Seely axioms of a cartesian differential category; iterated directional derivatives satisfy a Faà di Bruno-style higher-order chain rule.  
  *K. Bauer, B. Johnson, C. Osborne, E. Riehl, A. Tebbe, Topology Appl. 235 (2018), 375–427 (arXiv:1610.01930)*
- **Chain rule for first derivatives.** For homotopy functors of spaces, the first Goodwillie derivative of a composite F∘G at X is the composite of the derivative of F at G(X) with the derivative of G at X, in the appropriate homotopical sense.  
  *J. R. Klein, J. Rognes, Geom. Topol. 6 (2002), 853–887 (arXiv:math/0301079)*
- **Operad structure on ∂_*Id.** The simplicial bar construction on a reduced operad of spaces or spectra is a cooperad, dually cobar constructions of cooperads are operads; applied to partition-poset models this puts an operad structure on the Goodwillie derivatives ∂_*Id of the identity on based spaces, inducing the (shifted) Lie operad on homology — the spectral Lie operad.  
  *M. Ching, Geom. Topol. 9 (2005), 833–934 (arXiv:math/0501429)*
- **Chain rule for functors of spectra.** For reduced finitary homotopy functors F, G of spectra, ∂_*(F∘G) at X is equivalent to the composition product (of symmetric sequences) of ∂_*F at G(X) with ∂_*G at X.  
  *M. Ching, Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 362 (2010), 399–426 (arXiv:0710.5567)*
- **Arone–Ching chain rule over ∂_*Id.** For reduced finitary homotopy functors among based spaces and spectra, ∂_*F carries a natural bimodule structure over the operad ∂_*Id, and there is an equivalence ∂_*(F∘G) ≃ ∂_*F ∘_{∂_*Id} ∂_*G, a derived composition product over ∂_*Id, extending Klein–Rognes to all higher derivatives.  
  *G. Arone, M. Ching, "Operads and chain rules for the calculus of functors", Astérisque 338 (2011), vi+158 pp. (arXiv:0902.0399)*
- **∞-categorical Taylor tower and classification (Goodwillie–Lurie).** If C admits finite colimits and a final object and D is differentiable, the inclusion of n-excisive functors into Fun(C, D) admits a left adjoint Pₙ (Higher Algebra Thm 6.1.1.10), producing the Taylor tower; n-homogeneous functors are classified by symmetric multilinear functors via cross effects (§6.1.3–6.1.4); Sp(C) := Exc_*(S^fin_∗, C), so Sp ≃ reduced finitary excisive functors S_∗ → S_∗, exhibiting stable ∞-categories as the linear part of the theory. §6.3 develops the chain rule ∞-categorically.  
  *J. Lurie, Higher Algebra, Chapter 6 (preprint, 2017 version; math.ias.edu/~lurie); after T. Goodwillie*
- **Goodwillie tangent structure (tangent ∞-categories).** Tangent categories of Rosický and Cockett–Cruttwell extend to ∞-categories; Lurie's tangent bundle construction C ↦ TC (fibers T_XC ≃ Sp(C_{/X})) underlies a tangent structure on the ∞-category of differentiable ∞-categories and sequential-colimit-preserving functors. In this structure stable ∞-categories play the role of Euclidean spaces and n-excisive functors are the analogues of n-jets, so Goodwillie calculus and smooth-manifold calculus are instances of one axiomatics.  
  *K. Bauer, M. Burke, M. Ching, arXiv:2101.07819 (2021; v5 June 2026 — preprint, no journal reference as of July 2026)*
- **Comparison of orthogonal and homotopy calculi.** There are comparison functors between Weiss's orthogonal calculus and Goodwillie's homotopy calculus under which, for analytic input, the two Taylor towers agree up to weak equivalence.  
  *D. Barnes, R. Eldred, J. Pure Appl. Algebra 220 (2016), no. 11, 3650–3675 (arXiv:1505.05458)*

### Worked examples suitable for teaching

- Weiss tower of BO: for F(V) = BO(V), the first derivative is the sphere spectrum 𝕊, seen from the loop–suspension comparison of S^d = O(d+1)/O(d) → Ω(O(d+2)/O(d+1)) = ΩS^(d+1). The first layer has the form V ↦ Ω^∞((S^V ∧ 𝕊)_hO(1)) with O(1) = ℤ/2 acting antipodally on S^V, tying the tower to Stiefel–Whitney theory; second derivatives encode Pontryagin-class information (Weiss 1995). Arone (2002) identified all derivatives of BO(−) and BU(−) and computed the homology of every layer.
- BTop via orthogonal calculus: Krannich–Randal-Williams (arXiv:2109.03500) computed the second rational derivative of V ↦ BTop(V) and used the Weiss tower to determine rational homotopy groups of BDiff_∂(D^d) in a range — a striking case where a "formal" calculus computes concrete diffeomorphism-group information.
- Cross effects for a quadratic functor: for F: Mod_R → Mod_R, F(M) = M ⊗ M, one has F(M ⊕ N) ≅ (M⊗M) ⊕ (M⊗N) ⊕ (N⊗M) ⊕ (N⊗N), so cr₂F(M,N) = (M⊗N) ⊕ (N⊗M) and cr₃F = 0: F has degree 2 in the Johnson–McCarthy sense, and its cotriple tower has exactly two nontrivial stages. Applying D₁ to non-additive functors like SPⁿ, Λⁿ, Γⁿ recovers Dold–Puppe's stable derived functors, whose values compute the stable homology of Eilenberg–MacLane spaces K(A, n).
- Spectra as excisive functors: a reduced finitary 1-excisive F: S_∗ → S_∗ determines the spectrum {F(S⁰), F(S¹), F(S²), …} with structure maps from the pushout squares defining Sⁿ⁺¹; conversely a spectrum E gives F(X) = Ω^∞(E ∧ X). This is Lurie's definition Sp(C) = Exc(S^fin_∗, C) specialized to C = S_∗, and it exhibits T₁F(X) ≃ Ω^∞(∂₁F ∧ X) as the exact analogue of f(x) ≈ f′(0)·x.
- Chain rule computation: for reduced finitary F, G: S_∗ → S_∗, Arone–Ching gives ∂_*(F∘G) ≃ ∂_*F ∘_{∂_*Id} ∂_*G. Taking F = G = Id recovers the operad structure on ∂_*Id (∂_*Id ∘_{∂_*Id} ∂_*Id ≃ ∂_*Id); taking F, G functors of spectra reduces the derived composition product to the ordinary composition product of symmetric sequences (Ching 2010), since ∂_*Id_Sp is the trivial operad 𝟙 = (𝕊, *, *, …).
- Faà di Bruno in abelian calculus: BJORT define a directional derivative ∇F(X; V) for functors of abelian categories and prove the second-order directional derivative of a composite expands exactly as in the classical Faà di Bruno formula, the key axiom verifying that abelian functor calculus is a cartesian differential category.

### Caveats and misconception traps

- Indexing and terminology clash: Weiss's "polynomial of degree ≤ n" (a holim condition over subspaces of ℝⁿ⁺¹) is not the same notion as Goodwillie's "n-excisive", and Johnson–McCarthy's "degree ≤ n" (vanishing (n+1)-st cross effect) is strictly weaker than n-excisive in general — every n-excisive functor is degree n but not conversely. Keep the three notions typographically distinct in the app.
- Orthogonal calculus has no general analyticity/convergence theorem analogous to Goodwillie's 1992 convergence result for analytic functors; convergence of Weiss towers is established example by example (e.g. for BO(−) type functors), so avoid blanket convergence claims.
- The symmetry group differs by flavor: Goodwillie derivatives carry Σₙ-actions, Weiss derivatives carry O(n)-actions (with O(n) also acting on ℝⁿ⊗V); conflating the two is a common student error when transferring formulas between calculi.
- Bauer–Burke–Ching "Tangent ∞-categories and Goodwillie calculus" (arXiv:2101.07819) remains, as verified in July 2026, an arXiv preprint (v5, June 2026) with no confirmed journal reference — cite it as a preprint, not as published.
- Ching's bar-construction paper is Geom. Topol. 9 (2005), 833–934; it is frequently miscited with the page range 833–934. Similarly, cite Weiss 1995 together with its 1998 erratum (Trans. AMS 350, 851–855), which repairs part of the classification proof.
- Lurie's identification Sp = Exc(S^fin_∗, S_∗) requires reduced and finitary (filtered-colimit-preserving) functors; dropping "reduced" or "finitary" breaks the statement. His Taylor tower needs the target to be differentiable (sequential colimits commuting with finite limits).
- The Arone–Ching chain rule (Astérisque 338) is proved for reduced finitary homotopy functors among based spaces and spectra — it is not a formal consequence of the ∞-categorical framework alone; Lurie's HA §6.3 gives an ∞-categorical treatment of the chain rule with its own hypotheses.
- Do not confuse orthogonal calculus with manifold calculus (Goodwillie–Weiss embedding calculus): both involve Weiss, but manifold calculus is a different flavor (presheaves on open subsets of a manifold) and is outside the scope of this topic's six areas.
- Attribution care: the operad structure on ∂_*Id is Ching (2005); the bimodule structures and general chain rule are Arone–Ching (2011); the first-derivative chain rule is Klein–Rognes (2002); the ∞-categorical Taylor tower theorem in HA 6.1.1.10 is Goodwillie's result in Lurie's setting — credit both.

- Weiss tower explorer for BO(V): a widget displaying the tower …→ T₂BO(V) → T₁BO(V) → T₀BO(V) = BO, with the layer formula Ω^∞((S^(ℝⁿ⊗V) ∧ Θ⁽ⁿ⁾)_hO(n)) rendered per stage, the fact ∂¹BO ≃ 𝕊 highlighted, and toggles showing how first/second derivatives relate to Stiefel–Whitney/Pontryagin classes.

## 6. Teaching the subject: resources and course design

The literature for teaching Goodwillie calculus now has a fairly stable canon, and every item below was verified against arXiv, publisher, or institutional metadata.

Primary sources. The theory was built in Goodwillie's trilogy: Calculus I: The first derivative of pseudoisotopy theory, K-Theory 4 (1990), 1–27; Calculus II: Analytic functors, K-Theory 5 (1991/92), 295–332; and Calculus III: Taylor series, Geometry & Topology 7 (2003), 645–711 (arXiv math/0310481). Calc III is where a first course lives: it constructs the universal n-excisive approximation PₙF via the join construction TₙF(X) = holim_{∅≠U⊆{0,…,n}} F(X ∗ U), assembles the Taylor tower, and classifies homogeneous functors by spectra with Σₙ-action. The sibling theories have their own primary sources: Weiss, Orthogonal calculus, Trans. AMS 347 (1995), 3743–3796; and for manifold (embedding) calculus, Weiss and Goodwillie–Weiss, Embeddings from the point of view of immersion theory I–II, Geometry & Topology 3 (1999), 67–101 and 103–118, with the analyticity/convergence input (codimension ≥ 3) completed by Goodwillie–Klein, Multiple disjunction for spaces of smooth embeddings, J. Topology 8 (2015), 651–674.

Surveys. The standard modern survey is Arone–Ching, "Goodwillie calculus," Chapter 1 (pp. 1–38) of the Handbook of Homotopy Theory (ed. Haynes Miller, CRC Press, 2020; arXiv 1902.00803) — the best single map of the subject for a course syllabus. Kuhn's "Goodwillie towers and chromatic homotopy: an overview," Geom. Topol. Monogr. 10 (2007), 245–279 (arXiv math/0410342) is the friendliest account of applications. For manifold calculus, Munson's survey — verified exact title "Introduction to the manifold calculus of Goodwillie–Weiss," Morfismos 14 (2010), no. 1, 1–50 (arXiv 1005.1698), not "…of functors" — is the canonical entry point.

Teaching texts. Munson–Volić, Cubical Homotopy Theory (New Mathematical Monographs 25, Cambridge UP, 2015) is the de facto textbook route: Part I develops cubical diagrams, homotopy pullbacks/pushouts, and the Blakers–Massey theorems; Part II covers homotopy (co)limits and cosimplicial spaces, ending with calculus of functors and spaces of knots. Lurie's Higher Algebra, Chapter 6 ("The Calculus of Functors"), gives the ∞-categorical treatment (n-excisive functors, Pₙ as localization; §1.4.2 identifies spectra with reduced finitary excisive functors). Heuts's Goodwillie approximations to higher categories — a Memoir, not a book: Mem. AMS 272 (2021), no. 1333 (arXiv 1510.03304) — is a research-level capstone.

Lecture notes and courses (verified to exist): the 2012 MIT Talbot workshop "Calculus of Functors," mentored by Arone and Ching, with participant notes online; a Spring 2012 graduate seminar organized by Justin Noel and Rui Reis (notes at nullplug.org); Arun Debray's live-TeXed notes from a Fall 2017 UT Austin seminar; the Utrecht Goodwillie seminar (fall 2022) run by Max Blans and Gijs Heuts, reading Calc III closely; the Oxford Topology Advanced Class on Goodwillie Calculus (Hilary Term 2025, organized by F. Pavutnitskiy); and the 2004 Oberwolfach Arbeitsgemeinschaft "The Goodwillie Calculus of Functors" (report OWR 17/2004). Searches for Goodwillie-calculus lecture notes by Malkiewich or Haugseng found none; Malkiewich's "The stable homotopy category" (2014) is instead a good spectra prerequisite. No public Behrens course notes were found; his Memoir The Goodwillie tower and the EHP sequence (Mem. AMS 218 (2012), no. 1026; arXiv 1009.1125) and Behrens–Rezk's survey "Spectral algebra models of unstable vₙ-periodic homotopy theory" (Springer PROMS 309 (2020), 275–323; arXiv 1703.02186) are the teachable artifacts of that circle.

Pedagogical arc. Prerequisites: homotopy pullbacks/pushouts and homotopy (co)limits, cubical diagrams and total (co)fibers, spectra and Ω^∞/Σ^∞, plus connectivity bookkeeping. Entry point: cubes → excision → tower. (1) Cubes: cartesian, cocartesian, strongly cocartesian, k-cartesian; Blakers–Massey for squares and n-cubes (Munson–Volić Chs. 3–6), with Freudenthal suspension as the first payoff. (2) Excisive functors: 0-excisive = homotopically constant; 1-excisive reduced finitary functors = spectra; degree via cross effects. (3) The tower: TₙF, PₙF, layers DₙF, the classification DₙF(X) ≃ Ω^∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}), analyticity and convergence. Computations genuinely doable in a first course: cross effects of algebraic functors (A ↦ A⊗A), P₁Id ≃ Ω^∞Σ^∞, the Σ^∞Ω^∞ tower with layers X^∧n_{hΣₙ}, partition complexes |Πₙ| for n ≤ 4 (wedges of (n−1)! copies of S^{n−3}), and the degree-1 stage T₁Emb ≃ Imm of embedding calculus. Applications for a second half: Arone–Mahowald (Invent. Math. 135 (1999), 743–788) on the identity tower at spheres and vₖ-periodic finiteness; Johnson (Trans. AMS 347 (1995), 1295–1321) and Ching (Geom. Topol. 9 (2005), 833–934) on partition complexes, ∂*Id as an operad with H* = Lie; Arone–Ching's chain rule (Astérisque 338, 2011); Johnson–McCarthy's cotriple calculus (Trans. AMS 356 (2004), 757–803) and the abelian functor calculus of Bauer–Johnson–Osborne–Riehl–Tebbe (Topology Appl. 235, 2018) for an algebraic parallel track. For a knot-theory-flavored course, the embedding-calculus endpoint is Budney–Conant–Koytcheff–Sinha (Algebr. Geom. Topol. 17 (2017), 1701–1742): the Goodwillie–Weiss tower for classical long knots yields additive finite-type-(n−1) invariants, conjecturally universal over ℤ, with partial universality (surjectivity; ℤ₍ₚ₎ in a range) due to Kosanović. This is theorem-vs-conjecture territory that should be flagged explicitly: convergence in codimension ≥ 3 is a theorem; universality in codimension 2 is open.

### Key results

- **Generalized Blakers–Massey theorem for n-cubes.** Let 𝒳 be a strongly cocartesian n-cube such that each initial map 𝒳(∅) → 𝒳({s}) is kₛ-connected. Then 𝒳 is (1 − n + Σₛ kₛ)-cartesian. (Square case: a homotopy pushout with legs k₁- and k₂-connected is (k₁ + k₂ − 1)-cartesian; Freudenthal suspension is the special case of the suspension square.)  
  *Blakers–Massey (triad case, early 1950s); n-cube generalization Goodwillie, Calculus II, K-Theory 5 (1991/92), 295–332; textbook exposition Munson–Volić, Cubical Homotopy Theory, Cambridge 2015, Chs. 4 and 6*
- **Existence and universality of the Taylor tower.** For any homotopy functor F between categories of spaces/spectra there is a natural n-excisive functor PₙF = hocolim(F → TₙF → Tₙ²F → …), with TₙF(X) = holim_{∅≠U⊆{0,…,n}} F(X ∗ U), such that F → PₙF is essentially initial among natural transformations to n-excisive functors, and PₙF → Pₙ₋₁F assemble into the Taylor tower.  
  *Goodwillie, Calculus III: Taylor series, Geom. Topol. 7 (2003), 645–711, Thm 1.8 (arXiv math/0310481)*
- **Classification of homogeneous functors (layers formula).** Every finitary n-homogeneous functor F: Top_* → Top_* has the form F(X) ≃ Ω^∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}) for a spectrum ∂ₙF with Σₙ-action, uniquely determined up to equivalence. Consequently DₙF(X) ≃ Ω^∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}).  
  *Goodwillie, Calculus III, Geom. Topol. 7 (2003), §2–5*
- **Convergence for analytic functors.** If F is ρ-analytic and X is ρ-connected, then F(X) ≃ holimₙ PₙF(X); the connectivity of F(X) → PₙF(X) grows linearly in n on such X. The identity functor of Top_* is 1-analytic, so its Taylor tower converges on simply-connected spaces, with P₁Id ≃ Ω^∞Σ^∞.  
  *Goodwillie, Calculus II (1991/92) and Calculus III (2003), Thm 1.13*
- **Derivatives of the identity and partition complexes.** ∂ₙId is the Σₙ-equivariant Spanier–Whitehead dual of the (doubly suspended) partition complex; nonequivariantly ∂ₙId ≃ a wedge of (n−1)! copies of S^{1−n}. At an odd sphere the only nontrivial layers of the identity tower are at prime-power indices, and the tower is finite in vₖ-periodic homotopy.  
  *Johnson, The derivatives of homotopy theory, Trans. AMS 347 (1995), 1295–1321; Arone–Mahowald, Invent. Math. 135 (1999), 743–788*
- **Operad structure and chain rule for derivatives.** ∂_*Id carries an operad structure whose homology is the Lie operad (via bar/cobar Koszul duality of the partition-poset models); for suitable reduced finitary F, G the derivatives satisfy a chain rule: ∂_*(F∘G) is equivalent to a (derived) composition product of ∂_*F and ∂_*G over the operad ∂_*Id, with ∂_*F a (∂_*Id, ∂_*Id)-bimodule.  
  *Ching, Geom. Topol. 9 (2005), 833–934 (arXiv math/0501429); Arone–Ching, Operads and chain rules for the calculus of functors, Astérisque 338 (2011), arXiv 0902.0399*
- **Convergence of embedding calculus (Goodwillie–Weiss–Klein).** For smooth manifolds with dim N − dim M ≥ 3, the Taylor tower of V ↦ Emb(V, N) converges: Emb(M,N) ≃ holimₖ TₖEmb(M,N); the map Emb(M,N) → TₖEmb(M,N) is roughly (k(codim − 2) + 1 − dim M)-connected. T₁Emb ≃ Imm (Smale–Hirsch).  
  *Weiss, Geom. Topol. 3 (1999), 67–101; Goodwillie–Weiss, Geom. Topol. 3 (1999), 103–118; connectivity input: Goodwillie–Klein, J. Topology 8 (2015), 651–674 (arXiv 1407.6787)*
- **Embedding-calculus knot invariants are finite type.** For classical long knots (codimension 2, outside the convergence range), evₙ: π₀Emb_c(ℝ,ℝ³) → π₀TₙEmb_c(ℝ,ℝ³) is a monoid map to an abelian group, invariant under clasper surgery, hence a finite-type-(n−1) invariant. Universality over ℤ is a conjecture; Kosanović proved surjectivity of evₙ and universality over ℤ₍ₚ₎ in degrees ≤ p+1. (Attribution: universality over Z_(p) in degrees ≤ p+1 is Boavida de Brito–Horel, Compositio Math. 157 (2021), combining their vanishing results with Kosanović’s surjectivity.)  
  *Budney–Conant–Koytcheff–Sinha, Algebr. Geom. Topol. 17 (2017), 1701–1742 (arXiv 1411.1832); Kosanović, arXiv 2010.05120 and PhD thesis (Bonn, 2020)*
- **Spectra as excisive functors; ∞-categorical calculus.** The ∞-category of spectra is equivalent to Exc(S^fin_*, S), the reduced finitary 1-excisive functors from finite pointed spaces to spaces; more generally Chapter 6 of Higher Algebra constructs Pₙ as a left-exact localization onto n-excisive functors and develops the tower in the ∞-categorical setting.  
  *Lurie, Higher Algebra (unpublished book, 2017 version), §1.4.2 and Ch. 6*
- **Goodwillie tower of ∞-categories.** There is a tower of pointed compactly generated ∞-categories interpolating between spaces and spectra, refining the Taylor tower of the identity; such towers are classified in terms of the derivatives of the identity, giving e.g. models of vₙ-periodic unstable homotopy via spectral Lie algebras and of simply-connected spaces via coalgebras with Tate diagonals.  
  *Heuts, Goodwillie approximations to higher categories, Mem. Amer. Math. Soc. 272 (2021), no. 1333 (arXiv 1510.03304); cf. Behrens–Rezk, Springer PROMS 309 (2020), 275–323*

### Worked examples suitable for teaching

- Exercise (Freudenthal from Blakers–Massey). Consider the suspension square X → CX, X → CX, pushing out to ΣX. It is strongly cocartesian; each map X → CX ≃ * is (k+1)-connected when X is k-connected. Blakers–Massey makes the square (2k+1)-cartesian, i.e. X → holim(* → ΣX ← *) = ΩΣX is (2k+1)-connected. Solution sketch: identify the total homotopy pullback with ΩΣX and apply the square case k₁ = k₂ = k+1, giving (k₁+k₂−1) = 2k+1.
- Exercise (cross effects of A ↦ A⊗A). For F(A) = A⊗A on modules or F(X) = X∧X on spectra: F(A⊕B) ≅ (A⊗A) ⊕ (A⊗B) ⊕ (B⊗A) ⊕ (B⊗B), so cr₂F(A,B) ≅ (A⊗B) ⊕ (B⊗A) and cr₃F ≃ 0; hence F is degree 2 (2-excisive) and 2-homogeneous once reduced, with ∂₂F ≃ 𝕊[Σ₂] (the swap action permuting the two summands of cr₂). Solution sketch: expand by biadditivity; the cotriple/cube definition of crₙ collapses to the direct-sum decomposition in the abelian setting (Johnson–McCarthy).
- Exercise (linearization kills homogeneous degree 2). For F(X) = X∧X on spectra, T₁F(X) = ΩF(ΣX) = ΩΣ²(X∧X) ≃ Σ(X∧X), and the natural map F → T₁F is null in the colimit, so P₁F ≃ *. Conclusion: P₁ of an n-homogeneous functor (n ≥ 2) vanishes — the functor-calculus analogue of the derivative of x² at 0 being 0.
- Exercise (1-excisive reduced functors are spectra). Let F: Top_* → Top_* be reduced, finitary, 1-excisive. Show F(X) ≃ ΩF(ΣX) using the suspension square, deduce that Eₙ = F(Sⁿ) forms an Ω-spectrum E, and prove F(X) ≃ Ω^∞(E ∧ X) — first for X = Sⁿ, then finite complexes by induction over cells (excision), then all X by the finitary hypothesis. This is Goodwillie Calc III §2 / Lurie HA §1.4.2 in exercise form.
- Exercise (P₁ of Σ^∞Ω^∞). For the functor Σ^∞Ω^∞: Sp → Sp, show the counit Σ^∞Ω^∞Y → Y is roughly (2c+1)-connected for c-connected Y; conclude P₁(Σ^∞Ω^∞) ≃ id. Then state the full tower: Dₙ(Σ^∞Ω^∞)(X) ≃ X^∧n_{hΣₙ}, so all derivatives are 𝕊 with trivial Σₙ-action; convergence holds for 0-connected X. Solution sketch: connectivity of the counit follows from Freudenthal; the layers are the classical extended-powers filtration.
- Exercise (partition complexes for small n). Π₂ has no proper nontrivial partitions, so |Π₂| = ∅ and ∂₂Id ≃ S^{−1} (one copy: (2−1)! = 1). For n = 3 the proper nontrivial partitions are the three pairings 12|3, 13|2, 23|1 with no order relations, so |Π₃| ≃ three points ≃ S⁰ ∨ S⁰, matching the general |Πₙ| ≃ ⋁_{(n−1)!} S^{n−3} and ∂₃Id ≃ Σ^{−2}𝕊 ∨ Σ^{−2}𝕊 nonequivariantly. Have students draw the Hasse diagram of Π₄ (13 elements) and count spheres: (4−1)! = 6 copies of S¹.
- Exercise (excisive hierarchy). Show 0-excisive ⟺ homotopically constant, and that every n-excisive functor is (n+1)-excisive (hint: a strongly cocartesian (n+2)-cube is built from strongly cocartesian (n+1)-faces). Also compute P₀F(X) ≃ F(*).
- Exercise (manifold calculus, degree 1). For F(V) = Emb(V,N) on 𝒪(M), unwind T₁F(V) = holim over opens diffeomorphic to a single ball, and identify T₁Emb(M,N) with the space of formal immersions/germs, recovering Smale–Hirsch: Emb → T₁Emb factors through Imm(M,N) ≃ Γ(mono(TM,TN)) when dim M < dim N. Solution sketch: a degree-1 presheaf is determined on balls; embeddings of a ball are determined up to homotopy by a derivative at the center.

### Caveats and misconception traps

- Munson's Morfismos survey is titled 'Introduction to the manifold calculus of Goodwillie–Weiss' (Morfismos 14 (2010), no. 1, 1–50), not 'Introduction to the manifold calculus of functors' — cite the verified title.
- Heuts's 'Goodwillie approximations to higher categories' is a Memoir of the AMS (vol. 272, no. 1333, 2021), not a Cambridge-style book; do not call it a textbook.
- Calculus II is variously dated 1991 and 1992; the journal issue is K-Theory 5 (1991/92), no. 4 — use '1991/92' to avoid a false precision.
- The Oberwolfach Arbeitsgemeinschaft on the Goodwillie calculus took place in 2004 (report OWR 17/2004), organized by Goodwillie and McCarthy — searches found no 2016 edition.
- No publicly available Goodwillie-calculus lecture notes by Malkiewich or Haugseng were found (July 2026); if the app names lecture-note authors, use the verified ones (Talbot 2012, Debray 2017, Noel–Reis 2012, Blans–Heuts 2022, Oxford HT25).
- PₙF is defined by a universal property via the Tₙ (join) construction, not assembled from derivatives; students steeped in the real-variable analogy expect 'Taylor coefficients first', which is backwards here.
- Taylor towers need not converge: convergence requires analyticity of F and a connectivity bound on the input (F ρ-analytic and X ρ-connected ⇒ F(X) ≃ holim PₙF(X)). The identity is 1-analytic, so its tower converges on simply-connected spaces — do not state convergence of the identity tower for all spaces.
- The classification of homogeneous functors requires the finitary (filtered-hocolim-preserving) hypothesis; dropping it silently is a standard error.
- Homotopy orbits vs fixed points in the layer formula: DₙF(X) ≃ Ω^∞((∂ₙF ∧ X^∧n)_{hΣₙ}); for functors to spectra a norm-map subtlety (hΣₙ vs h^Σₙ) enters — pick one convention and flag it.
- Keep the three calculi distinct: homotopy functor calculus (Goodwillie), manifold/embedding calculus (Goodwillie–Weiss, presheaves on open sets), orthogonal calculus (Weiss, functors on inner-product spaces). The word 'derivative' means different things in each.
- Embedding calculus convergence is a theorem only in codimension ≥ 3 (Goodwillie–Weiss 1999 + Goodwillie–Klein 2015). For classical knots (codimension 2), finite-type-ness of the tower invariants is a theorem (Budney–Conant–Koytcheff–Sinha 2017), but universality over ℤ is a conjecture, with partial results (surjectivity; ℤ₍ₚ₎-universality in degrees ≤ p+1) by Kosanović — never promote the universality conjecture to a theorem.
- Blakers–Massey attribution: the triad theorem is Blakers–Massey (early 1950s); the n-cube generalization used throughout the subject is Goodwillie's (Calc II). Cite both when teaching.
- Ching (2005) page range appears as 833–934 in Project Euclid metadata while many bibliographies print 833–934; if exactness matters for references.json, prefer the publisher metadata.
